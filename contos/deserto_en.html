Just One More Desert to Cross

JANUARY 9, 2021 • AFROFUTURISM • SCIENCE FICTION

Dihya stared at the full moon. If she stared hard enough, would the Moon stop moving? Would it stay there, still, and the night never end? More importantly, would tomorrow never come? Just like when she used to watch the cake baking. "If you watch it, it stops baking," her mother would say, and she believed it. But that is not how it works. "Silly thoughts — you're not a child anymore, you're an ambassador and you have a mission," she murmured to herself.

The cold began to bite as the night wore on — and the Moon, obviously indifferent to everything, marched across the sky. Cold had not been expected on that summer night. Her clothes — just trousers, a thin sweatshirt, and a hijab — would soon be insufficient. In any case, it was useless to prepare for the weather. It could snow, turn infernally hot, the sea could rise and swallow the city, or dry up forever. Everything was unpredictable; everything was possible.

Sitting there, she had a sweeping view of the ancient ruins, now an archaeological site embedded in the city. Further on, she could even see the Mediterranean. She could not help but notice the beauty of the scene: the icy wind blowing against her face, the stone structures corroded by time, the sound of waves rocking her mind, and up above, nearly outshone by the full Moon, several colored dots. The Satellite Cities, being built by thousands of frantic, tireless workers — invisible from that distance. A perfect scene between present and future.

Ah, the Satellite Cities — humanity's wager. Dihya knew it was an inefficient solution, very inefficient. They needed to do more. She would do more.

"Dihya, I found you!"

The woman did not need to turn around to recognize the voice. It was Aníbal, her younger brother. So grown up — a full beard, broad shoulders. How had he changed so fast?

"I didn't know I was hiding, brother. Peace be upon you!" She smiled as soon as he sat beside her. Worried and anxious as she was, her brother always made her smile. He made everyone smile.

"And upon you, peace. You weren't hiding? You always come to these ruins when you're upset or anxious."

"For dates, too — but I never told you that."

"Only if you were dating rocks. What are you thinking about, sister?"

"The usual — a heap of 'what ifs.' What if our city hadn't been destroyed thousands of years ago by the Romans, but instead Carthage had won? What if our ancestral kingdom of Numidia had resisted every form of intrusion? What if it had been Egypt's emperors marching into Europe, stealing their monuments? What if we hadn't been the stage for Europe's and the Ottomans' disputes? What if the peoples of our continent had found the Americas on their own, before being enslaved — arriving there as free people?"

"The past, sister. Carthage didn't win, but here we are. Regardless, all of humanity is equally condemned, and that's why you only need to worry about tomorrow. Your big day, huh?"

"It's about tomorrow, too, brother. I don't know if I'm the right person. You've always been the one looking toward the future. You and your odd habit of building perfect miniatures of Explorer Vessels. I remember when you built a kind of spy drone and hid it at school. Mom was furious, sure you'd be expelled! But lucky as you are, you were hailed as a genius. And now look — Chief Engineer of the Afro-Arab Space League. I've always looked at ruins... I didn't even notice you becoming a man."

"Don't worry, Dihya. You stepped away because you had to, and tomorrow you'll do what needs to be done. Remember what Dad used to say. Our ancestors crossed the desert with no certainty at all. It's just one more desert to cross. If there's any future that worries me, it's dinner. Mom made couscous. There — I knew that would cheer you up!"

***

The hum of the thrusters always made Dihya slightly dizzy. She could admire the vessel — all the engineering and technology behind it — but certainly not with the same enthusiasm her brother showed in these situations.

She had taken two anti-nausea pills before boarding. Hundreds of flights, yet her body never seemed to adjust. If this was how it felt flying through the atmosphere, imagine space, with no reference point at all? Would it be worse, or better?

"Good afternoon." Dihya greeted the pilot as she boarded, testing her helmet microphone.

Putting it on over a cap worn over her hijab, which in turn covered her long hair, was a genuine ordeal. A hot, sweaty ordeal.

Not to mention these United Space Force vessels were always tiny and warm. It seemed as though air-conditioning did not need to be powerful in the cold of space. And sitting in a cockpit built for a co-pilot? The seat certainly did not recline. The briefcase she carried — her mission — would have to sit on her lap. Why hadn't they sent a normal commercial jet, if her flight was just a simple trip?

"Good afternoon, Madam Ambassador." The pilot responded without much attention.

She was busy, meticulously checking the flight plan and the controls. Dihya always admired how pilots were so bureaucratic in their tasks, nimbly running their fingers over the touchscreen controls, checking every element of the ship, every blinking light, repeating "checked," "checked," "checked."

"Flight Control, this is Captain Dandara, code 342, Standard U.S.F. Vessel. Departing Tunis, destination: Johannesburg. Estimated flight time six hours and twenty minutes, arrival at 20:00 local. Cruise flight autonomy of thirty-one hours. Weather conditions unpredictable. Weapons operational. Mission: transport of the Ambassador of the Afro-Arab Space League."

Weapons operational? On a transport mission? Given the flight schedule, Dihya would miss the hour of Maghrib, the sunset prayer. Some feeling told her she would have much to be thankful for in this prayer. She would pray with double devotion — if she didn't throw up first.

The Ambassador did not hear Flight Control's response to the pilot — they were probably on a separate channel. Fair enough; the rising noise, the bluish glow from the thrusters, the trembling of the vessel, and the sign of the cross the pilot made all indicated they were ready for takeoff. She was uneasy about the peculiarities of this flight but did not want to show it. As ambassador, she was always being observed, so she would wait patiently. The truth always surfaced. Her mission was beginning. She could not turn back now, anyway.

Shortly after takeoff, once the vessel reached cruising speed, sleep came. Aircraft had always been an excellent sedative for the ambassador. The anti-nausea pills only reinforced the inevitable.

Hours passed before Dihya woke, still in flight, probably roused by the sudden need for a bathroom. All that morning coffee, sipped while reviewing her work, was exacting its toll.

"Captain Dandara. Excuse me. May I make a request?" She spaced her words carefully. It was hard for Dihya to speak during flight. All her strength went to fighting the nausea.

"Yes, Madam Ambassador. Welcome back. Please, feel free." The pilot responded through the helmet mic.

"I know we have a schedule and a flight plan, but could you fly a bit lower and slower, just for a moment? I'd like to look at the scenery." It would be a good distraction, she thought.

Dihya, as ambassador, was accustomed to visiting every country and continent, and to observing how rapidly landscapes were changing. It was like a snowball. The changes had begun slowly, centuries ago, with the first industrial chimney, the first forest felled — and then they accelerated, accelerated, until they became an avalanche.

"Yes, Madam Ambassador, but please be aware that at a lower altitude the vessel will be hotter and bumpier."

"That's fine. It'll only be for a little while."

"Confirmed. Descending to 2,900 feet above ground level."

Desert. Desert where a forest should have stood. Just five years ago, she was certain a forest still existed there. They were passing over the equatorial region. She didn't even know the name of that patch of barren soil she was staring at. It seemed as though all the continent's deserts had finally merged. Perhaps all the world's deserts. Sand, clay, and dust floated on atmospheric currents across the globe, and would continue to do so until the entire planet turned to sand or ice.

Her mission was more urgent than it seemed. She felt the weight of the briefcase on her lap. She looked at the pilot through the windshield's reflection. How old could she be? About twenty-five? Hard to tell behind the uniform.

The pilot noticed she was being observed.

"Ma'am, if I may say so — I chose to transport you. When I heard about the opportunity, I volunteered. They wanted to send a rookie pilot, fresh out of training. But I insisted on taking part."

"Is that so?" The ambassador always found it amusing when someone implied she was famous. She could not get used to being a public figure.

"Yes. My family and I have listened to several of your speeches. What you did — that political bridge between the Arab League, the North African League, and the Sub-Saharan League — was incredible. Creating a single, unified league. Truly incredible. It inspired me to join the U.S.F., to look toward the stars."

"Look toward the stars." She would certainly get along with Aníbal.

"Captain, if you don't mind my asking — where are you from? I didn't recognize your accent." They were conversing in French, a language inherited by some from the bygone days of colonization, and one of the U.S.F.'s official languages.

"I was born in Angola. As a child, we moved to Brazil, but now I live wherever the U.S.F. stations me. I must have been through about five countries by now."

Angola and Brazil — both ravaged by the Great Fire, which had swept through nearly every tropical region of the planet a decade ago. It was one of the catalysts, among many, for the hastened Satellite Cities Project.

"Captain, you who see the Satellite Cities being built up close — what do you think of the idea?" But that was not the question she wanted to ask. What she really wanted to ask was: "Will there be room for everyone?" "Who will be chosen to migrate up there?" "What will it be like to live confined, watching the world die from above?"

Before Dandara could answer, an alarm sounded. The ship's lights switched to red.

"Pirates." Dandara informed. "Three of them, in pursuit. I'll try to make contact. One moment."

The ambassador turned her head as far back as she could. As the vessel banked, she caught a glimpse of the other craft. They were slightly larger, a model cobbled together from parts of various aircraft, including pieces of U.S.F. vessels — shot down or stolen.

"Flight Control. This is Captain Dandara, code 342. We are being pursued by three unidentified vessels. Visual contact confirmed, no response to verbal contact. Transmitting location and situational radar. Awaiting instructions."

Moments later, Dihya heard several shots, watching through the small window as the glowing tracers of incandescent projectiles traced arcs while the vessel maintained its zigzag.

"Flight Control. Confirmed. Warning volley fired. They have not diverted. Continuing evasive maneuvers. Awaiting instructions."

The same bureaucracy as when powering up the ship. Trained pilots depended on it, to keep from panicking and making mistakes. Dihya was most certainly panicking. She was grateful the co-pilot cockpit was inoperative, or she might have caused an accident — for she did not foresee what the pilot would do next.

Dandara yanked the stick fully to the right while simultaneously reversing the directional thrusters 180 degrees. Dihya, nearly fainting, had no idea this was even possible — and it seemed the pirates didn't either. The maneuver sent the vessel into a wide spin, flipping it through a full rotation. More incandescent projectiles, and an explosion. Just like that, the pilot had reduced the number of pursuers by two.

She passed between them as they scattered in opposite directions. Had they been trained, they would probably have maintained course and launched a counterattack.

Dandara then began to climb, pitching the nose up, fractions of a second before the pirates opted for sharp turns — each one in a different direction. Dandara was performing a vertical inversion. She was not only climbing but tracing an inverted, curving arc. The vessel was beginning to turn upside down.

Dihya hated roller coasters, and in a vessel at combat speed, the G-force sensation was crushing. Blood was being pushed into her brain. She needed a bag, urgently.

One pirate was still completing its turn. Since Dandara's maneuver had been far tighter, she achieved an attack angle the moment she began her descent — still inverted. It was as though Dandara already knew the speed, the curve, the other pilot's reaction before he even chose to attempt the vertical inversion. More shots — and one fewer pirate.

"Ha! I love that maneuver. I like to call it the half-moon — you know, like in capoeira?" The pilot exclaimed, clearly thrilled by the situation, oblivious to the G-forces.

Dihya could not share her excitement. She knew her life depended on theirs being taken, but she understood their motives — the extreme poverty, the desperation. She wished the last pirate would give up.

"Flight Control. Two pursuing vessels downed. Wait... The last one is opening a channel, surrendering. Yes, thank you. Really? A new record — two vessels in forty-five seconds? I'm pleased, but the previous record was mine too. Awaiting instructions. Confirmed, I will continue the transport mission. Instructions: last pursuing vessel cleared to retreat."

"Retreat?" the ambassador asked, surprised.

"We don't have time to escort him, and it would be dangerous besides. He could call for reinforcements, while we, well... We don't have much personnel down here. U.S.F. policy is to take prisoners, but when that's not possible, we let the enemy flee. Better than the alternative."

The red light in the vessel suddenly went out, and Dihya released the armrests, which she had not even realized she had been gripping fiercely.

She knew piracy had intensified. Groups lurked near the great terrestrial cities being dismantled for raw materials, ever since all natural deposits had been exhausted. Mainly European and Asian cities, built with African ores stolen centuries ago, now being taken apart and shipped into space — literally. But she had not known piracy had spread to deserts, since, well, there was not much to steal there. Most likely a desperate measure, to grab whatever they could. She suspected the U.S.F. knew about the pirates and was withholding the information from the public. They were losing control.

She couldn't fall back asleep, but at least she didn't throw up.

***

There were hundreds of protesters. The ambassador was escorted from the car to the entrance of the United Space Leagues headquarters, passing through a gauntlet of signs — some favorable, most opposed to the controversial Satellite Cities Project. She was exhausted; it was night, and she'd had time only for a prayer and a quick shower at the hotel after landing.

She was glad to have been accompanied by Captain Dandara herself. Now, without the helmet, mask, and flight suit, she could see that the pilot, with her enormous colorful braids and a wide smile, looked even younger.

A new generation. Were the next generations doomed to live in artificial cities, under constant threat of war between exhausted nations, amid desperate pirates? She pressed her briefcase to her chest. There are alternatives.

The building was modern — a monument to the promised new era. In the great circular assembly hall, with its tiered benches, seating was assigned by lottery. The dome was glass: a vast screen displaying an animation of the night sky, highlighting the Satellite Cities as if seen from various locations on Earth.

Finding her seat, she was pleased to learn she would be sitting next to the ambassador of the Latin American League — a Brazilian named Paulo. Better yet, a Brazilian woman. Dihya needed to remember the representative preferred feminine pronouns. On the other side sat the ambassador from Israel. She could not remember his name.

She was not in the mood to mingle with the various other delegates forming cliques, standing around, laughing together, taking photos, exchanging cards. Nor did she feel like exploring the adjacent room with its coffee and assorted refreshments, where she would probably have to socialize. There were moments when that was necessary — and she was very good at it. But today she needed to concentrate.

It was very strange to see how at ease those people felt inside, while outside the police pushed back hundreds of protesters with riot shields.

Five more minutes until the Congress began. "Focus, Dihya, focus." Should she review her presentation once more? Go over her speech? Better not. In any case, she noticed Paulo approaching.

"Good morning, Madam Dihya!"

"Good morning, Paulo — you've... You've changed! You look lovely."

"Yes — now you and no one else will get the pronouns wrong. By the way, is it my impression, or has your Spanish improved?"

"Yes, I've been studying. We're seeking a rapprochement with the Latin American League."

"That look on your face! So tell me — what did you think of the trip?"

Odd question, Dihya thought.

"An adventure, you might say."

"I heard through some sources that your League is planning something new." Paulo spoke with a mysterious air. "You know, with my smile and good Brazilian cocktails, other ambassadors tend to say more than they intend. Through them, I discovered your Afro-Arab League is hiding something new, and it's unsettled quite a few people."

"Well, it was a secret project because it was still in its early stages. Even within the League, few people knew. Today it will be announced. We were simply waiting for the right moment." Dihya was not particularly surprised that some rumor had spread. Even less surprised that a new project had unsettled others. The Satellite Cities were a gold mine, and any innovation could be a threat.

"Then you have much to thank me for," Paulo said, playfully.

"How so?"

"I told the U.S.F. that a radical anti-space-exploration group had threatened all the ambassadors. It's not entirely a lie, but it forced them to think about our transport in a more... cautious way. I did it because I thought you, my friend, might be in danger."

"Yes, I was indeed attacked by pirates. Come to think of it, the situation was strange. Perhaps they weren't even pirates." Dihya could have been angry, but she was only sad.

A chime sounded. The Congress would finally begin. Slowly, the participants began to take their seats — except the ambassador from Israel. He would be the first to speak.

Israel was ahead of schedule. It was one of the few countries that had decided to build a Satellite City on its own, without joining a League. They had finally completed the basic structure, and the first families of volunteer colonists would arrive that very week.

After the congratulations and wave of hope, the Congress followed its expected course. The ambassador could barely concentrate, drawn back to the surface of her attention only by Paulo's ironic remarks.

She caught only fragments of discussions about shortages of materials, the rise of popular unrest, ancient and senseless intrigues between Leagues — or even within them. She heard about an unforeseen sandstorm that appeared during the takeoff of a large freighter, which crashed onto an entire dock in Australia. A major loss of material and lives for the U.S.F.

Her attention truly returned only when her name was called. It was the invitation to take the floor.

She nodded to Paulo and looked around. She was not the same woman who, hours earlier, had been terrified inside a vessel. She was the pilot now, and this was her space.

She walked calmly to the center, where a small podium stood.

"Good afternoon, ladies and gentlemen. Peace be upon you. We have been holding these Congresses, meetings, conferences, and accords for years. I am glad the Satellite Cities project is indeed progressing toward temporary success."

Voices, murmurs, exclamations, and protests. The word "temporary" was something that unsettled everyone. A taboo among all present.

"Yes, temporary. Today we are dismantling entire cities in search of raw materials. In the future, we may strip every stone from the planet and reassemble it. But there will be a limit. A limit of space smaller than what our population requires."

"Madam Ambassador, with all due respect, this has been the subject of discussion for decades. For decades, no other model has been conceived. Is it truly productive to bring this issue to the floor again, with so many more urgent matters?" It was the ambassador from Israel.

"Thank you, Mister Ambassador. As with the other presentations, there will be time for debate afterward. Therefore, I ask that you not interrupt me, just as you were not interrupted. You will see that you spoke too soon." (What was the maneuver Dandara pulled? Half-moon. That's it — you're about to receive a half-moon, my dear, Dihya thought, with a glint in her eye.) "Here is your answer."

Dihya opened the briefcase and drew out a small sphere. A holographic projector which, once activated, displayed a large spaceship in three dimensions. By the scale, it would be enormous — larger than three or four Satellite Cities. Voices, murmurs, exclamations, and protests.

"We all know habitable planets exist — we have known since humanity launched the Explorer Vessels. We all know that migration has always been a constant for our species. Some voluntary, others forced by disasters, invasions, wars, or slavery. Ladies and gentlemen, in this regard, as ambassador of the Afro-Arab League, I must say: we have a great deal of experience on this topic."

While many showed open astonishment, Dihya saw Paulo flash a thumbs-up.

"You can, of course, turn back. Go against the current of the history that defines our species and retreat to those elitist caves called Satellite Cities. Or you can go further. Beyond the stars. The great obstacle to that was a gap in the model for complete Terraforming. Without it, any colony would be merely another Satellite City, like all the rest, set down on a strange and hostile planet. Well — a team headed by the Chief Engineer of our League has presented a broader solution. We present a new project. A project to seek new planets and make them habitable. And in so doing, to be free to migrate across space."

At last, the audience fell silent.

"Now that I have everyone's full attention, I can show the details. Please, put on your virtual reality glasses — they will be synchronized with this projection. Excellent. Now look for the file labeled 'Project Carthage,' and prepare to learn how to cross a desert."
