The Old Woman, Winter, and the Mountain

AUGUST 22, 2022 • FANTASY • SHORT STORY

Aja Han had seen many strange people climbing the trail of Mount Kaya. Never, however, an old woman who exuded so much suspicion.

It was not uncommon for men to brave the arduous path that passed behind her house — the last in the village, and she suspected, perhaps the last in all the northern reaches of the Empire.

All manner of pilgrims passed through. People such as Monks of the Celestial Way, their heads shaved and foreheads dyed orange, sometimes carrying the faint scent of incense. Or priests of the foreign religion of the One God, in worn-out robes and wooden sandals, whose steps echoed dryly on the stone. They tested their spirits and perished in their bodies. Aja had curiously noticed that the visitors were always men, and they always came in summer. Even those outsiders deemed mad by the locals — even they respected the Mountain.

And so, since it was not every day that something new happened in those parts, Aja could not help but follow the old woman.

Inside her head, her mother's soft voice warned, "don't be rude," and in the rough syllables of a strong accent, her father reminded her, "don't forget to pick the Blue Herbs for supper."

Above those voices, the girl conversed with herself.

The date marking the end of the yellowed autumn and the start of winter was a time of gathering and shelter. Of course one couldn't let an old woman wander alone in such a dangerous place! Her parents would understand; her curiosity would transform into kindness — or at least that was how her mischievous side tried to fool her good sense.

That is not quite what happened. As she crept closer to the woman, Aja realized that perhaps this had not been a good idea after all. The woman, who fell more into the category of "antiquity," possessed certain... strange traits. White hair that gleamed as though it held moonlight within. Fine garments — was that a nightgown? — that offered no protection against the cold. A strange and agile ability for a lady who appeared hunched over.

The worst part, however, was the feeling that things along the trail were not as they should be, and Aja knew the place well enough to notice. The trees swayed in one direction while the wind blew in another. Pale, sporadic lights illuminated the path, even though the Sun was hidden behind a thick mantle of dark clouds.

Better go back, it'll be dark soon, the girl convinced herself. As for the strange woman — well, she concluded she should tell her father about her, perhaps omitting a few impressions that would be dismissed as exaggerations, and perhaps before the first snows fell the village men could find her body.

Back at home, Aja realized that even when she tried, something prevented her from speaking about the woman — to her father, to anyone. She tried speaking to the wind, but her voice failed. Since supper was soon served and the smell of roast lamb hung in the air, the girl forgot what had happened. The memories gradually faded like smoke.

It was time to celebrate the coming of Winter, and as they feasted on food and warm wine, the Han family could not imagine how severe it would be.

2 – Winter

Winter advanced and closed its fist upon the North of the Empire. The world outside no longer existed; it was a suspension of reality, a void filled with cold and snow. All that existed in the Universe was Aja's house, orbiting in isolation through the white void, with the Han family huddled around a colossal oven and its welcoming heat.

That is why it was nearly impossible to believe that someone was knocking at the door. It did not seem possible for life to exist out there.

"Could it be a bear?" suggested Aja's younger brother, as he wrestled with a runny nose.

Aja made a face at him, and, full of assurance, explained that bears do not knock on people's doors in a rhythmic and polite fashion.

The boy took a deep breath, ready to argue, but was silenced by the swift rising of their father, who raised his arm and closed his fist. His two children had never been trained in the hunters' sign language — they were too young — but the message was clear to anyone: shut up.

The father walked to the door. Aja thought he should have taken the bow and arrow, even if it would have been useless at such close range. Damn it, a staff would have been better than nothing.

The door was opened and two enormous human forms were revealed. Aja thought they were visitors from the Celestial Kingdom or some other Astral plane. The appearance of those visitors was unlike anything Aja had ever seen.

The contrast between the two black silhouettes and the white landscape was striking. Not only were the heavy garments and the strange hat dyed a profoundly dark color, as if absorbing all light, swallowing the glow of the fire — but the very complexion of the two people was as well. Only an enormous crest of the Bear Emperor in Royal Yellow, stamped upon each chest, stood out.

In a way, Aja thought her brother had been right. But these Bears were more dangerous. She knew that crest could only be borne by officers of the Empire. There were soldiers in the region who claimed to represent the Empire. They were nothing more than local teenagers with a thirst for power who soon discovered they had none, much less reinforcements. The region was too poor and too far to attract the capital's attention. The inhabitants resolved everything on their own, as it had always been.

Aja understood, then, that these outsiders were special. She had learned that in the Empire there were people with skin and hair of every color; she knew of Black people in the far South, in the Falcon Province, but she had never seen anyone like them.

And in the peculiar Southern accent, with vowels that seemed swallowed, one of the visitors spoke calmly:

"Mr. Cian Han? How about you let us in? You wouldn't want your house to get cold, would you?"

Aja's father made room at the door and only closed it once he saw that the two figures had seated themselves before the great oven. Then he sat on the other side, facing them.

After a long and awkward silence, Mrs. Han, Aja's mother, who had until then been lost in her own thoughts, remembered her social graces and forced a smile.

"Forgive me — you must be tired. Would you like some tea? Our bread will be ready soon, we were preparing supper... Oh, how careless of me — what are your names? Or... sirs or madams, forgive me, with all those winter clothes, it's hard to tell."

"Don't worry. We are both, and we are something more. Use whichever pronoun you like. And dinner won't be necessary — we expect to be brief here." One of them replied, still in that velvety voice.

"Mr. Han, we've been told you have lived here for thirty years." The other stated immediately, in the same voice. In fact, Aja noticed they were alike in every way.

She wanted to know if they were twins, or if everyone in the Southern Province looked the same. If everyone there was as tall, or if that was just their trait. She wanted to know more about how they had arrived. Why their garments, so dark, reflected none of the fire's glow. Why they hadn't removed their boots and dried their feet, as anyone would. And how they had walked on soft snow without snowshoes. And why their eyes were so still.

She held her curiosity. If even her younger brother was behaving, she would not be the one to seem like a nosy little girl.

"It seems you were well informed." Her father's voice pulled Aja from her reverie.

"Thirty years. The same length of time our Lord Bear Emperor has unified the Empire. And your accent is from the... Crocodile Province. A beautiful land of Marshes — don't you miss the warmth?"

Mr. Han did not answer. He merely furrowed his brow, staring at the two. Aja had never seen her father so sharp with travelers. Many times the Han family had fed pilgrims and offered a warm bed — a last comfort of civilization for those who would likely never return. On such occasions, it was not uncommon for visitors to request a specific burial rite, "just in case and if it's no trouble," they would say. Some preferred to be buried, others cremated, some simply left for the carrion birds. It didn't matter — Mr. Han provided.

"Were you a fisherman?" the second visitor continued, as though this were a friendly conversation.

"Not everyone there was a fisherman. I built sampans and canoes."

"Were? Not anymore?" one of them asked, with a sly smile.

"I think he's speaking of the time before the floods." The other visitor answered.

Aja knew her father was from the Crocodile Province, which was why she had always taken an interest in the region. She never knew why her father had moved, but she knew he could never go back. Nearly the entire Province and its main villages had been flooded by the construction of great dikes and dams fifteen years ago. It was a grand project of the Bear Emperor to control the floods of the Thousand-Headed Serpent River — the river that was born there, in the mountains, and flowed to the edge of the Empire.

The girl had been a baby when it happened, so she did not remember the propaganda pamphlets of the "Bear Who Swallowed the Serpent." The downstream regions, now with the river controlled, had become arable in ways never seen before. The vast flooded rice fields ensured ample food production, made available to the imperial armies, which easily subdued any rebel provincial forces — typically poor and starving.

That was why her father, stung by the comment, bristled.

"I'm certain you didn't come here to talk about my past. Say what you want."

One of the visitors replied.

"Thirty years in this province, thirty years at the foot of this mountain. You must know it quite well."

"Well enough to know if something strange is going on. Have you seen anything strange on the mountain?" the other completed.

Mr. Han wrinkled his brow. It seemed he had not been prepared for this turn of conversation.

"No, nothing." He answered. After a moment's thought, he added: "This winter is very harsh, of course, but we'll survive."

"Forgive us, Mr. Han. We should have been more direct. What we want to know is whether someone unusual has passed through here. Perhaps you've seen? Or someone in your family?" For the first time, the visitors seemed to notice the other people in the house, and Aja felt a chill as she was stared at, as though a frozen current whispered in her ear. She averted her gaze by instinct.

"No. Nobody here saw any—"

Aja barely heard her father's voice, transported to forgotten memories. An old woman, on the mountain path. Could it be? When? It was a feast day; she remembered the smell of lamb. Had she dreamed it? The images came from behind a curtain of fragile, thin silk, trembling toward oblivion. The girl, on impulse, before the rest of the image could fade again, said:

"I think I saw a woman, perhaps before the Longest Night supper. I'm not sure." Am I?

"Here, poor girl. Look into my eyes. And remember."

The visitor's voice echoed in Aja's mind. It was all that seemed real. And then the girl saw a hunched old woman walking hurriedly along the road, with hair like light. The memories came raw, as if leaping into a frozen river and the water engulfed her completely, flooding her nose and mouth. She choked.

And then, she told them everything.

Her father looked at her as though he had never known his own daughter. The two visitors seemed genuinely pleased.

"What is your name, girl?" one of them asked.

"Aja Han."

"Very well, Miss Han. You will climb the mountain with us. Prepare yourself."

3 – The Mountain

There was the sky, shining and pulsing like something alive, adorned with points of blue and white light — lights that reverberated, flickered across many dimensions, and moved across the firmament, defined by darkness at its edge, where it met the mountains on the horizon. It seemed within reach, so high were they, above the world. The mountain seemed to exist as a path to it.

A clear sky had not been expected, but Aja had not expected many things that were happening during those nights. She had followed in silence for nearly two hard days the two imperials — enormous and majestic — who walked upon the soft snow without leaving footprints.

Now and then, she would stop, her eyes turning backward, her chest tight with a silent uncertainty. She wanted to continue — she felt she could do it — but she wanted to return to the warmth of her family. She wished her father had followed her, protected her, but she knew he needed to stay with her mother and her younger brother.

They stopped beneath an enormous boulder, which would make a good shelter against the icy wind. The girl leaned against the rough rock. The pace of the march was quiet, but harmonious. She knew when they would stop to hunt — and they always returned with something — when they would melt snow to drink, and when they would build a fire and make camp.

"Miss Han, how far do you think it is to the summit?" one of them asked. They frequently asked the girl for instructions, directions she thought best. Aja always answered, relying on her experience, but also on intuition.

The girl considered. She wasn't certain where they were — there were several possible ways up the mountain, different paths — but she imagined they were still less than halfway.

"At this pace, and if our luck of clear skies holds, perhaps a week. Twice that, if it snows."

They seemed to trust the girl. She decided to ask a question. Her first in days.

"You both seem to know mountains well. You know where to step." What she really wanted to ask was how they walked without leaving footprints.

"Miss Han, we know that is not what you wish to ask. Tell us — what do you see?"

"What do you mean?"

"What do you see that's different? What seems strange, out of place?"

"To start with, you two," she thought.

"The old woman is a mystery, and you're certain she's alive and here. No one could survive this long on this mountain." The girl realized that perhaps even she no longer believed that second claim.

The two sat in silence, looking at Aja, waiting for more.

"And there are other things. This clear sky. The fire in our campfire doesn't seem... natural. There are trees with dry leaves, as though it were still autumn. And I saw river orchids near a stream. The stream should be frozen, and the orchids only appear in late spring. And I've been following a path, as though something were dictating my directions, without knowing why."

"Most people would not notice any of that. In fact, neither would we, despite our training. That is your gift." One of the imperial officers replied.

The other added.

"There are people like us, with certain gifts. Call it magic, if you like. Many of them never realized it; others used it for personal gain; others joined as mercenaries or volunteers in rebel armies."

Aja knew this, but had always thought the stories were exaggerated. That these "magical" people were simply people who were good at what they did. She knew that the Bear Emperor, for precisely these reasons, had hunted, imprisoned, or exterminated those who might be dangerous.

As if sensing the girl's thoughts, one of them replied.

"Perhaps we should introduce ourselves. My name is Kiar. My gift is perceiving people's feelings. A flash, a spark that lets me understand what passes through others' minds. My sister, Niar" — So they are sisters! Aja had guessed right — "possesses more practical abilities. She is exceptionally skilled in combat. Beyond any training."

The girl realized then that the old woman must have her own gifts, and that she was being hunted.

Kiar continued.

"And the old woman, yes, she has gifts. Don't think that the Bear Emperor is evil for banning shamans, mages, sorcerers, or whatever you wish to call these people. Many are recruited, trained by the Empire itself, and kept loyal. The woman we seek is dangerous. She was born in the Ghaak Desert, in what is now part of the Lizard Province. As a child, she discovered she could conjure rain upon the scorching sands. She used her gifts to maintain oases and establish stops for the silk and trade routes from the East. Soon she became rich and powerful, unifying the desert peoples. When the Emperor ascended, she was a fierce commander of enemy troops, allied with foreign governments."

The girl did not reply, absorbing the news. So she had a gift, and without realizing it, was already being used by the Empire.

***

The clear weather allowed them to see the other peaks of the mountain, a sea of white needles pointing toward the Gods, in a spectacle of orange glitter touched by the rising Sun. Aja estimated they were already many meters above the smaller peaks, and now there were only two routes to the main summit.

"Are you sensing anything?" Kiar asked, stopping beside the girl, looking in the same direction she stared. She saw nothing.

Aja thought about saying it was strange the sky was clear, but she didn't want to seem like someone who doubts her own good luck. A clear sky did not feel like a threatening spell.

"We can take advantage of the calm winds and go by the southern route — shorter. The problem is there's a rock face that needs to be climbed. Or we go southwest — an easy path, though longer."

The twenty-meter rock face was not terribly difficult to conquer; her own father had installed iron bolts as handholds, and the sisters were no ordinary beings. At that altitude, in that thin air, any person not born in the mountains would feel at least a crushing fatigue. The two outsiders were relentless, whether by magic or force of will.

"How long for each route?" Niar asked.

"By the southern ridge, a few more hours. We'll reach the base of the rock face by nightfall, and only that separates us from the summit. By the southwest route, four or five more days to the summit, depending on the accumulated snow."

"Let's take advantage of the clear skies." Niar decided.

The path, as expected, was swift. Aja still found the ease unsettling, but she thought that perhaps the old woman was sleeping, tired, or more likely, dead.

She faced the rock wall with disguised pleasure. She had conquered the mountain; she was at the final step. She had been climbing that face since childhood — the mountain was her backyard. Being in winter was just a detail.

She didn't care about the reason that had brought her here, whether she was being used by the Empire or not. What mattered was that each bolt conquered, each meter climbed, signaled the solidity of her drive.

"I'll go first." Aja signaled. Despite the bolts, she cautiously pulled from her pack her piolets — small ice picks she had learned to use as soon as she had learned to walk. And bolt by bolt, she began to climb.

The girl was halfway up the face when a strong breeze passed over the back of her neck. The wind whispered magic and menace.

"Hurry!" She shouted. A powerful sense of urgency surged within her, even before she understood why.

Aja began climbing faster, increasingly enveloped by a growing wind. Had night not been advancing, she would have noticed the sky blanketed by dense clouds. A snowflake struck her forehead, then another and another. Soon she was in a blizzard, a furious tempest of wind and ice.

The wind reached ballistic speeds, like an arrow sent by the Gods, aimed at the trio exposed on the rock face. The old woman knew; the old woman had waited, Aja thought.

She tried to look down and managed to see Niar, whose head nearly touched her foot, and a bit lower, a fading silhouette of Kiar, who seemed to sway in the wind, clinging to the rock with only one hand.

Aja realized the bolts holding them were coming loose — not driven deep enough to bear her weight against the wind. With a shock, she understood that perhaps the bolts had been deliberately loosened. A perfect trap. The old woman had waited for the right moment.

Aja drove the piolets into the mountain. She had climbed this way before, and the summit was close. She could not stay still — that was certain death — she needed to reach solid ground.

Niar seemed able to follow her. Even without bolts or tools, her skill was astonishing; she gripped each stone, wedged her foot into each crevice, like a mountain cat. Aja could no longer see Kiar; perhaps she was too far below, the snow and fog concealing her. She tried to shout, but the wind scattered her words the instant they left her mouth.

Tears sprang to her eyes — from shock, from guilt. She shut them for an instant. How had she not seen it was a trap? Hold on. Don't let go. Don't fall.

Aja was trembling — a mixture of cold, rage, and fear. She knew every inch of the rock face; she could drive the blade into stone like the skilled hand of a musician playing a piano, a chef seasoning a feast.

She climbed to the top. She always noticed Niar, who followed her like a shadow. They both reached the plateau of the summit. Aja realized minutes had passed, and Kiar had not come up. She paused for a moment, hopeful, breathing deeply the scant air available, her lungs filling with frozen air, but her heart warming with fire.

When she realized Kiar would not come up, she fell to her knees. Holding back tears, she faced Niar, who stood like a marble statue before the old woman.

The old woman.

Aja noticed the ancient sorceress, who regarded the pair with an expression of veiled pleasure. She was very thin; only tufts of hair remained on her head. Several fingers were missing from her hand; one foot seemed to be half gone. The mountain had exacted its price.

"Three climbed the mountain, only two made it." The sorceress's voice was rough and good-humored. "I should have used a stronger forgetting spell on you, girl, but I was afraid of frying that little head of yours."

Niar, in a voice colder than the mountain, announced:

"Kátira Altoz, by order of the Emperor, I have come to kill you."

"Oh, of course you have. And after me, you would kill the girl's father? Two birds with one arrow, as they say down South."

Aja stared at Niar. Could it be true? Yes, it was.

The girl realized her father had known the old woman was on the mountain — that was why she knew the rock face, that was why she had set the trap. It was possible that this had been her father's own idea.

Niar ignored the girl. She was there for one thing only. She unsheathed a knife from her belt — the same one she had used many times to cut the small game during their nights camping.

The blizzard, which had weakened, returned in full force, swirling around the three figures there, on the roof of the world.

"It's no use — that crazed magic of yours won't stop me. Your magic has gone mad, like you. It's out of control; it was easy to follow your trail. Did you really think you could hide?"

Aja could feel the old woman's magic. It was not crazed; it was not that of someone in hiding. There was purpose, there was will.

The blizzard ceased. The girl did not even see the knife being thrown. The weight of the sharpened blade, the speed with which it cut the air, the angle of its arc, defeated the snowstorm. The old woman had the metal buried in her heart, but a smile on her lips.

Aja then understood. She saw why her father had helped the old woman, and why the sorceress had chosen that mountain. All that snow, accumulated, waiting for spring, waiting to melt and pour into the great Serpent River, forcing, collapsing, destroying the dikes. The flood would bring famine; innocent people would die; thousands would lose their homes. But there would be deserters, civil wars, revolts — the Empire would not hold. Would knowing this help her? Just as the old woman and her father had chosen, would she sacrifice innocents?

Niar turned to the girl.

"You can come with me and be trained by the Empire. Or you can stay — I don't care."

"I'll stay. The mountain is my place." She answered, without hesitation.

Author's Note: This is my longest tale so far — and not by accident. It was written for a selection that required a relatively high minimum length, at least by my standards. It was a stimulating challenge. I confess that R. F. Kuang's writing had a great influence on this work. I wrote this story as an exercise, a test to incorporate some of the techniques I learned from her, and also to experiment with the longer format. I also sought an ending with some kind of dilemma.
