Sanitary Protocol Seven-Beta

MAY 13, 2025 • SCIENCE FICTION

When the Lieutenant unlocked and pushed open the hatch of the decompression corridor, the first thing we saw was not the expected emptiness, but a Cyborg. Upon noticing our presence, he pointed a weapon at us.

We froze. We weren't military. Not really. I mean... we'd done the training, but never had any practice. Our reaction was more shock than instinct.

It took a few seconds for the fear to dissolve into strangeness. That was no weapon. It was a white, smooth piece of equipment, with a little green light blinking in no hurry. He aimed it at the Lieutenant's bewildered forehead.

"Body temperature: thirty-six point five degrees Celsius." The Cyborg said. Then added, as if needing to explain: "That means you do not have a fever."

We were even more confused. This station, the Esmeralda, was supposed to be... offline. Empty. Like so many others, hastily abandoned years ago, in that time when the universe seemed intent on silencing humanity.

Only now were we beginning to reactivate the trade routes. Our mission was simple, more archaeological than military: inventory what remained of the structures left exposed to the void.

The Lieutenant, with the patience of someone who had dealt with far worse space bureaucracies, hesitated, processing what to say. She chose, in the end, courtesy.

"Uh... thank you. For the reading? May we come in?"

But the Cyborg seemed immune to politeness.

"Unfortunately, entry denied. None of you are wearing a mask. Nor are you respecting the minimum interpersonal distance of three meters."

The Lieutenant blinked, snapping back to reality.

"Yes, well... we noticed the station was already depressurized, with the air recycling system running. That's why we're not wearing suits... We thought..."

"Even so," the Cyborg interrupted, its robotic voice relentless, "Protocol Seven-Beta, sub-item three point four, requires the use of a surgical mask or equivalent in shared enclosed environments, regardless of pressurization and atmospheric recycling."

The Lieutenant put a hand to her forehead.

"Good lord... the pandemic is over. It's been over three years. You've been on this whole time?"

The Cyborg hesitated. There was something almost human in that brief silence. Its eyes — or the sensors that stared back at us — blinked slowly, confused.

The Lieutenant then turned to me.

"Grant him access to the ship's network, all right?"

I consented with a nod. The moment I authorized it on the tablet, it was instantaneous. The Cyborg's eyes blazed electric blue. For a few seconds it received the update — those last three and a half years poured into its memory. Unfair, I thought. To dump upon it so many memories from a time that most of us are trying to erase, layer by layer.

Abruptly, the blue glow ceased. It straightened up with a metallic sound. It lowered the white device and moved aside, clearing the doorway.

"Welcome to Station Esmeralda," the voice slightly less metallic now. "Please be informed that our restaurants and lodging services remain deactivated. However, sealed beverages and supplies with unexpired dates may be purchased with standard credit at the self-service unit on Level Two. We appreciate your understanding and wish you a pleasant stay."

We entered. The station was a ghost of itself, but not a frightening ghost. It was a sad ghost. One could hear the drone of autonomous machinery. The echo of our footsteps was the only sound the place chewed on. The lights settled upon empty tables and counters, finding no human face. On the floor, there was only the dry trail of leaves, fallen from completely withered plants. The air drank of itself, recycled too many times over, with a faint taste of nothing at all. I felt a knot, right there, where longing gave way to claustrophobia.

Behind us, I saw the Cyborg move with robotic precision. It pulled out a bottle and began meticulously spraying hand sanitizer on every surface where our hands might have rested. Protocol Seven-Beta. Relentless.

***

Note: This tale was born from an anthology by Mia Couto, on "the small diseases of eternity," which contains some stories about the Covid pandemic. I thought it was an interesting theme, one I had been avoiding writing about, but which had a profound impact (and a lot of good things came out of it... the ending of the Locklands series by Robert Jackson Bennett, the book "The Kaiju Preservation Society" by Scalzi, among others).
