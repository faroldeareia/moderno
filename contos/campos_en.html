Tulip Field

JUNE 4, 2019 • SCIENCE FICTION • SHORT STORY

They adapted the Aerospace Support Base in less than a month. In truth, after the signing of the peace accords that ended the War of the Three Systems, everything was changing rapidly.

Personally, the greatest of all changes would begin here, in the former Annex Armory 1B, now titled "Reconditioning Center."

There were many of us waiting to be called to the small service windows, with their provisional partitions. Many standing — there were not enough benches for everyone — others sitting in the corners against the walls, chins resting on their chests.

I have known this enormous hall since its construction. I myself helped build it. Back then, we packed it with enormous stacks of ammunition, mechanical parts, assorted crates, and massive conveyor belts running to the Cruisers. So different now... After decades, how would the worlds live in peace?

Ticket RC4005, Window 230. My turn.

The man who attended me was a civilian, elderly, with a tired look. He must have been exhausted. Even so, he began the conversation with a smile.

"Good afternoon, sir. Welcome. Would you like a brief explanation?"

I knew my answer would not matter. By law, he was required to relay the situation. That way, no one could claim ignorance. It was a test, too — to gauge how compliant we were with what was to come. I simply nodded. He continued:

"Very well. Under the Peace Treaty, general military demobilization is mandatory, by mutual agreement among the three federations." After a brief pause, he went on, syllable by syllable: "No exceptions. Do you understand?"

"Yes, sir." Two words, so simple to utter, drilled into my mind after so much conditioning.

He nodded. Then continued:

"And this, obviously, includes you." He began reading from a tablet. "According to the report here, you belonged to an Engineering Battalion."

"Yes, sir," I answered again. So repetitive.

"Then I imagine you'd like to continue in your field... Construction, I mean. What do you think?" His gaze now met mine.

I was surprised, though I did not show it. I had not known there would be a choice. And what difference would it make? This false notion of freedom was deliberate. In any case, I reflected for a long moment on what to say. My hesitation visibly bothered him.

"During a mission on the allied planet Tohil, I saw great fields of tulips. I thought: what planet grows flowers during a war? I'd like to go there, if possible."

The man wore a look of astonishment. He thought we had no emotion. Well, he would have been right. But in truth, I had never stopped thinking about that place, and I never knew why.

"Of course..." He typed something on the screen, glancing up at me. "I see here that in the tulip fields they use large machinery for sowing... We can certainly recondition you to be one."

So that would be my fate. Or rather, the fate of my parts. An android built for war, sowing fields of tulips.

"Yes, sir," I said, for the last time.
