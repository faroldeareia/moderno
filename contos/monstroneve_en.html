Monster in the Snow

JANUARY 11, 2026 • FANTASY • CHILDREN'S

In the depths of a forest where winter reigned without end, there lived a creature with no name. It was tall as a young pine and white as freshly fallen snow. It broke the ice so the salmon could breathe, dug up frozen nuts to feed the squirrels, and picked the sweetest berries for the newborn birds.

One day, the silence of the forest was broken by barking and heavy footsteps. A human family had arrived in the region, carrying flint arrows and the smell of campfire.

While setting up camp, the father found a footprint in the snow. It was deep and wide, three times larger than a bear's paw. He felt fear tighten in his chest. He called his children, his voice grave: "Never stray from the tent. There's something out there, something immense. If the footprint is like this, imagine the beast."

The next morning, the parents needed to go out hunting, leaving the children with strict orders to stay by the campfire. But the children's curiosity was untamable, and the forest whispered its invitations. When the parents left, they defied the warning. They went out to play, just for a little while, they thought. But the forest was a labyrinth of white and gray. Within minutes, they were lost.

The giant found them huddled beneath the root of a pine tree, their skin turned blue from the cold. It did not roar. With a gentleness that did not match its size, it gathered them into its warm, furry arms. The children, feeling that kind warmth, stopped crying. It carried them to its cave, sheltering them from the cutting wind.

When the parents returned, they found only emptiness. Outside the tent, they saw their children's small footprints vanishing into the woods, and right beside them, the colossal prints. "It took them!" the mother screamed, tears freezing on her face. The father grabbed his spear, eyes consumed by fury and fear. The sky darkened and a blizzard began to howl.

Fighting against the snowstorm, guided only by the panic of losing what they loved most, the parents followed the trail to the mouth of a dark cave. The blizzard lashed at their backs, heightening the urgency. They needed to be swift. They needed to be lethal.

Inside, the giant slept, its chest rising and falling slowly. In the dim light, they saw intense red stains on its white fur. Blood, the father thought, and his heart raced. Fear guided the spear; there was no hesitation, only the dry sound of metal tearing flesh. With one precise and tragic blow, the monster drew its last breath without even waking.

Then the children's voices broke the silence. "Daddy! Mommy!" The children emerged from the back of the cave, warm and alive, but their eyes filled with tears when they saw the fallen guardian.

"Why did you do that?" the older boy cried, hugging the creature's lifeless paw. "He saved us from the cold! He brought us here because the blizzard was coming." The girl pointed to the red stains on the monster's fur and then showed her own hands and mouth, also smeared with red. "He gave us the best blackberries in the forest to eat... he was just stained with berry juice."

The parents dropped their weapons, falling to their knees on the cold cave stone. The wind outside continued to howl its mocking song, but inside the cave the silence was absolute. They looked at the gentle giant the forest had lost, understanding too late that the real monster that day had no claws or fangs; the real monster had been the blind fear they carried within themselves.

***

Note: This tale can have two endings, depending on the audience's age. In the version for younger children, the children's voices appear before the fatal blow. The father raises the spear, but the children arrive in time to prevent the tragedy. Both versions teach about not judging by appearances, but the first offers hope and redemption, while the second, as written here, explores the irreversible consequences of fear and hasty judgment. This story was born from an improvised telling for my three-year-old son. Naturally, with the optimistic ending!
