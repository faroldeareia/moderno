Mermaid

JULY 21, 2021 • DRAMA • SHORT STORY

As a child, she would lie on the sand and hum, letting the salty pulse of the sea wash over her body again and again. She only left when her mother's sharp call — a dissonant note in the chorus of waves — summoned her, and her father began folding up the colorful beach umbrella. Then the girl, a knot of salty stubbornness, would murmur that life away from the water was a mistake, and that she was not leaving.

"Don't be fussy, it's late! You're not a mermaid." Her mother's voice, dry as sand under the midday sun, tried to anchor her on solid ground.

Ah, but if the cruel architecture of reality decreed she did not possess a silver tail, then perhaps she could fly far away and be an astronaut — exploring boxes around the house, walking about in a makeshift helmet.

"No, seriously — what do you want to be?" The adults' question hung in the air, demanding that infinity fit into small, limited boxes.

A star. A poetess. A heroine whose heart beat to an epic rhythm. And being nothing — was that an option? It was not. She discovered, with the cold prick of wisdom, that this choice did not exist on the world's forms.

The teachers said she was good at science. Perhaps an inventor? Her name would go down in history; she would win prizes.

But despite all of that, life — an artisan of its own whims — had other plans for her. It chose her to be a homemaker. And she loved her family, every thread of that complex fabric of affection and routine. Even though sometimes — well, many times — she felt lonely.

A loneliness that grew when her children, now adults, left home to build their own. It worsened when she forgot where they lived, their names, even the simple arithmetic of how many there were. Where had she put the prescription for her medicine?

Amid the crumbling fog of days, there was no surprise when they took her on a trip. An unknown little girl, calling her grandma, held her hand, leading her along paths she no longer recognized, all the way to the edge... yes, to the edge of the sea.

When the water touched her, she finally forgot what her mother had said. The voice that had insisted she was not a mermaid dissolved with the white foam of the sea.

She felt the familiar salt water on her silver tail, savored the beloved summer Sun on her amphibian skin. With a sigh that tasted of salt and of home, she let the sea carry her away.

***

Author's Note: This story was born from a memory that is not mine, but my wife's. She was fifteen when she traveled with her grandmother to the beach. She remembers that experience as the last moment when her grandmother was still, in some way, whole. Though there were already gaps in memory, small absences. After that, Alzheimer's erased what remained.
