Special Sunscreen

APRIL 1, 2021 • SCIENCE FICTION • SHORT STORY

As soon as we made the transfer near Saturn, boarding the astro-line bound for Earth, my daughter's clothes began to draw attention.

Or perhaps it was not just her clothes, but her very features — so much like her mother's: dilated pupils, a peculiarly shaped nose, the unique texture of her hair, and that complexion only the descendants of colonists from distant star systems possessed. She did not notice the involuntary attention, focused entirely on the details of the journey. She seemed intent on memorizing the complete route map, with the stations and their names blinking above one of the disembarkation doors — Jovian Moons, Jupiter High, Residential Rings, Ceres Mining — mentally timing the interval between each stop. Decades earlier, when I made this journey in the opposite direction, I had done a similar calculation.

I noticed her lip moving slightly as she murmured the station names in a language she practiced only with me. It was her first time traveling this far, to the so-called "end of the line," as all the rest of humanity, scattered across the stars, called our own home planet.

She did not seem to mind the increasingly frequent Earthlings on the transport; after all, I was already a specimen of one. Since she was a baby, my daughter had had plenty of time to squeeze my cheeks, poke me, and tug my hair with a rare curiosity and a sincere laugh.

Perhaps that was why only I could perceive the stares of those Earthlings watching her, imagining what they secretly thought. Did they mentally call her "spacer," "colonial"? Or worse terms?

"You're going to run? Going to be one of those cowards who abandon Earth and breed aberrations and monsters across space?" That was the last question my father asked when I told him I would accept a permanent job in the agricultural colonies at the edge of the Local Bubble. I never answered him — about that, or anything else.

"What's with that face, Dad? Is your stomach acting up?" my daughter asked, finally noticing me.

I merely smiled and confirmed. I had taken several anti-nausea pills; still, the hypervelocity travel played tricks on my body. It would make a good excuse to disguise my feelings.

"Look, no use complaining! Coming along was your idea!" she continued. At some point during adolescence, my daughter had chosen to seem perpetually irritated.

"I won't complain, and I won't throw up on you. Although, given the baby you were, it would only be fair payback."

She made a sound through her nose that was her adolescent version of displaying a kind of revolt, as though I didn't deserve so much as a word. But I sensed her smiling discreetly. She was tense too.

"Mom said this was the first time you've been back since you left. What do you miss?" She changed the subject abruptly, to my surprise. It was not like her to take an interest in personal things about me — at least not in recent years.

What could I say? The nights drinking and then suffering the hangover? Simply sitting outdoors and feeling the Sun — irreplaceable? The sheer amplitude of an entire planet, knowing that anywhere you went you could breathe or walk freely, without a dome or a safe zone confining you to the impassable?

Before I could answer, a voice announced in several languages that we were arriving at Earth Station. In reality, it was a space station at some geostationary point above the south pole.

From there, we would need to catch transport to a landing station on Earth or to any of the hundreds of satellite cities orbiting the planet.

My daughter had insisted I rent a private transport and go straight to the University, where she needed to complete her enrollment. She wanted this done before going to the hotel, eating, or anything else. She made the request as though her life depended on enrolling before all else, even though there were weeks left before the deadline.

As the door of the Space-Train opened, I held my daughter's shoulder.

"Jata, stay close to me," I advised. "The station is packed with pickpockets. Hold the bags tight and avoid these crowds." I tried to speak in a clear tone that conveyed the seriousness of the situation.

As soon as we rose from our seats, I positioned myself behind her. While we were seated, only stares reached her. Now that we would be walking, I feared some random, more curious and disrespectful hand. She did not seem to mind or notice the added protection.

As we disembarked, Jata stumbled stepping from the transport onto the platform. I heard her exclaim one of those new slang words I didn't know.

"I've done this route dozens of times in virtual reality, but I didn't imagine it would be like this!" She tried to speak loudly, craning her neck back so I could hear her over the other people talking and vendors hawking illegal transports, travel packages, and products I had never seen.

"What? Look, we need to go down four floors, to the transport rental companies. All right?"

To my satisfaction, the way down was without major incident.

While an employee at the rental shuttle company ran the final inspection on the hull, before having me sign a liability waiver, a complete view of the planet appeared through the station's transparent ceiling. I could finally answer my daughter's question.

"The sea."

"What about the sea?" She made a face that suggested I was turning into a crazy old man, bringing up random subjects.

"I miss the sea. Or rather, the beach. Before I left, they were closing all the remaining beaches. The contamination levels in the ocean were too high, something like that. They were going to try a new cleanup procedure and thought it best to shut everything down to avoid side effects. That was the last straw that made me decide to accept the job offer and move."

She didn't answer and stared at the enormous blue sphere above us. I doubted that, from this astronomical distance, her mind could perceive and understand what a beach was. The colony where she was born barely had lakes, and none of the colonies she had visited had large surface bodies of water.

***

The air traffic was as bad as I remembered, but it was not hard to retrace the route to the University, where I landed in one of the large parking lots — now mostly empty, reflecting the rest of the campus. You could even hear the trees swaying in that contemplative atmosphere.

During enrollment season, in the middle of the break, the place felt more like an enormous park, with a few students arriving by public transport to the registration sites, then promptly leaving for parties or exploratory strolls through the buildings. The nostalgic feeling was overwhelming, as though I had never truly left that place, and all the rest of my life had been a temporary commotion. Nothing on campus seemed to have changed; even the old, peeling paint on the surrounding buildings appeared eternal.

For my daughter, the feeling was the opposite. She not only did not know the place, but the entire planet was foreign to her — with a sensation of... fullness, of being overwhelmed. Even a handful of dirt here held more biodiversity than the distant moon we called home. At least for now.

She checked the documents again. Something she had already done hundreds of times at home, on every interstellar vessel, and then on the Space-Train through the solar system. I wanted to tell her those documents were just a formal redundancy. The unique identification each of us had embedded in our skin would contain everything needed about our legal life — but she already knew that.

"All right, Dad, I'm going in!" she announced, after a long breath.

"You sure you don't want me to walk with you?"

"Of course not!"

"Fine. But is your communicator on? Is the volume up?"

"Yes, Dad... You don't have to be so worried." She turned her ear and showed me a discreet communicator near it. "I even fitted this direct link here."

"Good. I'll be waiting around here. If you decide to go out with people, let me know and I'll head to the hotel and pick you up later. No hitchhiking." I knew the most exciting part of enrollment was meeting future classmates and socializing. How I missed that!

She stepped out of the shuttle with determination, heading toward the enormous building that would be part of her identity forever.

I took advantage of the free time to turn on the radio and listen to local music. Apparently, nearly three decades had not been enough to change Earthlings' musical taste. Lost in the music, I was startled when my daughter tapped on my window, signaling me to unlock the small shuttle.

"That was a quick enrollment," I said, casting a line, wanting to explore how it had gone.

"Yes."

I figured from her curt reply and sad expression that things had not gone very well. What could have happened? As I opened my mouth to ask, she simply told me to leave, saying she was tired.

On the way to the hotel, I remembered there was a good bar in the area. Without warning, I changed course and landed in a nearby lot.

"Where are we?"

"I want to show you a place!"

"No, Dad, let's go to the hotel, please."

Then I pointed to an open bar, one of those with several chairs on the sidewalk and a hand-painted sign.

"I used to play music here on Friday nights. Let's have some local beers. You're not going to deny your old man this rare pleasure, are you?"

Perhaps the novelty of drinking with her father for the first time, or the curiosity of knowing where I had spent my nights when young, got her out of the shuttle, dragging her feet.

As soon as we sat down, once again my daughter drew attention. At another table, a group of four men stared at her and whispered among themselves.

I tried to ignore them and focus on a cold beer. It was not just strange to be drinking on Earth again, but to be doing so with my daughter, sharing part of my past with her.

We toasted.

"Ugh, this is horrible!" she said after barely touching the glass to her lips. Her grimace reinforced her opinion.

"You get used to it. Though maybe it's best you don't get too used to it."

She looked around.

"So you used to bring girls here?"

"Your mother would probably ask exactly that question. But yes, I did. It's a good place. It gets much busier at night — or at least it used to. I'm amazed it still exists with the same name, even though the menu has completely changed."

Now the men were speaking louder, and we could make out a few words like "sample," "exotic." I wanted to stand up, to respond, to punch. As if sensing my agitation, my daughter stared at me, startled. She whispered something sad, but I could barely hear.

"Look, honey, I understand the university offered an excellent scholarship. But you can try to get into others. In other places, you know? There's no need to rush. You know that, right?"

"Maybe, Dad, I'll think about it. But the program is really good — it's not just about the scholarship. This was one of the first places to pioneer Stellar Meteorology, and you know I've always been fascinated by it."

"Yes, I remember that at eight you already knew the classification of most stars in our entire quadrant. No idea where you got that from. All right then, but don't push yourself too hard. We'll always be home regardless."

***

When Jata called me, my first instinct was to say her mother wasn't in. But she began the conversation directed at me, saying she wanted to show me something. I hid the embarrassment of my near-blunder and showed my curiosity.

"You'll never guess where I am!" she said, truly excited.

The camera was very close to her face, so I couldn't see the surroundings. But I found it odd that she was wearing huge dark sunglasses and a black hat that came down and covered most of her face.

"No guesses. Where?"

"At the beach." She pulled the camera back and showed the white sand, the ocean in the background, and several empty kiosks. "It's my first time!"

I could see she was even wearing long sleeves, as she twirled to give me a panoramic view.

"And how is it? You're all armored up! Haven't gotten used to the Sun?" I asked casually.

"Yeah, no. I don't have sunscreen for me, you know how it is."

Her answer was a little shy. I understood that her skin and hair might be far more sensitive than I had initially thought. The special creams and sunscreens for outsiders were very expensive and hard to find, since each colony had a different biotype and few visited Earth. So the products were nearly artisanal, made to order. I made a mental note to perhaps send her more money, since she had always been stubborn about asking for financial help.

"But the best part is I found out the sea access has been reopened. The Mequian nanobotic geoengineering projects finished faster than the planned schedule — you know, the project has a feedback intelligence that even the inventors didn't expect to be so efficient," she continued, cutting through my reverie.

A few months at the university and she was already using terms like "Mequian nanobotic geoengineering" and "feedback intelligence," whatever that meant.

"Did you like the famous salty Earth water?" I preferred to talk about swimming.

"I didn't go in. And that's actually what I wanted to talk about. How about you and Mom come here and we all go in together? What do you think?"

My next holiday was nearly a year away, and from that moment I began counting every minute. I was thrilled at the prospect of visiting the sea with my family; it would be a unique experience, a feeling I couldn't have previously imagined.

"Absolutely!"

"Great, I have to go now. Kisses!"

***

Our excitement for the trip only grew with time. We used to travel quite a bit when Jata was a child, to distant, off-the-beaten-path places in a quadrant of the galaxy where bolder colonies were still being inaugurated.

But given the choice, Jata always preferred a small planet called Ormuz and its enormous, gigantic waterfalls — even though they were methane and acidic. That she chose a coastal town near a mountain range, where she could enjoy not only the sea but several waterfalls — this time suitable for swimming — was expected. But the scenery was far more beautiful than I remembered being possible on Earth.

I was sitting in the living room, waiting for my wife and daughter to get ready so we could head to the sand, when I began remembering Jata calling me for this beach trip, all bundled up and covered. I was wearing only swim trunks, wondering if I shouldn't put on a shirt or something to look less exposed and not stand out from the rest of my family. Or was I trying to find an excuse to hide my now-aging body, to disguise the belly that had finally grown out of control, among other marks of age? I caught myself, surprised by such self-consciousness at my age.

While I was still making up my mind, my daughter appeared. She wore only a bikini and a crochet hat that served more as a fashion accessory than protection.

"Dad, is the shuttle unlocked? I forgot something on the seat."

"Yes, it is." And then I remarked: "Good to see you found sunscreen for yourself."

She was inside the shuttle, distracted, and answered casually.

"What sunscreen, Dad? Oh, phew — I found the waterproof makeup!"

She rushed back to the bathroom. When she emerged, she had marked her skin in the unmistakable style of the colonies, accentuating nearly every detail that set her apart from me and other Earthlings. Even the color of her bikini was a perfect contrast to her complexion — so much more resilient than mine. I finally remembered that the star irradiating over our colony, unlike most others, was far more powerful than the Sun. Now that I was on the planet, this was so obvious.

Perhaps that was the first time I truly saw my grown daughter, the roles reversed — when she was teaching me more than I was teaching her. I looked down at my trunks and at my belly, comparing my posture with my daughter's. A year had passed since that phone call inviting us here. I shrugged.

"Jata, do me a favor? Can you rub this plain old sunscreen on my back?"

***

Author's Note: This is a short story, and it was also written for a magazine submission (and was not selected!). My idea was to write about returning home after a long time away. This is because I, personally, no longer live in the city where I was born, and it feels increasingly strange to go back. I used this same premise for another story, "The House with a Lemon Tree," which I may even like more than this one.
