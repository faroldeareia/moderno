The Grapes Left Behind

NOVEMBER 6, 2019 • SCIENCE FICTION • SHORT STORY

Half the people stared at me with hatred, the other half with curiosity. But no one could ignore my presence — not in that lime-green vest, representing the most controversial migration policy in the history of civilization.

I did not care about other people's judgment; I was concerned with only one interlocutor: my grandfather. Before leaving, I had asked myself whether I should go in plain clothes or in uniform. My wife tried to convince me to be discreet. Perhaps out of sheer stubbornness, I took the risk.

The answer to my decision came when I found my grandfather at the central spaceport. He looked me up and down, lingering on my clothes, and smiled ironically, in silence. He did not ask how I was, whether the vessel had rattled coming through the atmosphere. He did not ask whether the pressure difference had made me sick, did not ask whether my wife or his grandchildren — inhabitants of another world — were well. He treated me like a stranger.

We boarded the subway and rode to a distant station. Anguished, I tried to start some conversation — any — that would not spark conflict between us.

"Station's pretty empty, isn't it?"

"Yes, of course — you people sent nearly everyone away," he said curtly.

That was that; there would be no easy dialogue. I thought it better not to respond. In silence, I rented a manual car from the kiosk at the station. Nobody drove anymore, not even here on Earth. It was my grandfather who had taught me to drive, and I missed the gritty wind blowing through the window, the country music in the background, the feeling of control as the car swayed along the mountain road.

"You never could drive well, could you? It's like you've gotten worse. Not many cars for you to practice on up there, huh?" my grandfather said, masking a smile.

I smiled too.

"No, up there all transport is collective. There's no space to waste on avenues and roads..."

His sadness returned abruptly. I waited nearly three hours to hear his voice again.

"Pull over — we're entering the farm. I'll get the gate."

I braked and turned my gaze to the landscape. There were thousands of grapevines around us, filling the valley. My grandfather returned to the car carrying a few bunches of grapes. They caught the weak sunlight — smooth and soft. They looked sweet.

I felt like a child: small and wordless before that green sea, touched by its unique, special scent, in awe of that immensity gifted by the universe. There was nothing like this beyond Earth. I remembered the juices and wines Grandpa and Grandma used to make, the many grape sweets. We would eat until we couldn't stand it. We would sneak glasses of wine until we stumbled.

***

We were drinking homemade wine on the veranda of his house. Against the starry sky, a bright blue sphere stood out to me. From that distance, it was about the size of a fist. It was neither the largest nor the brightest of the several similar spheres. But I would recognize that particular one from anywhere.

"Look, Grandpa — that blue dot, can you see it?" I pointed. "That's where we live. Right about now, my kids should be coming home from school."

He merely nodded. That man rarely left that piece of ground, let alone the country, or traveled to the Outer Cities. His abrupt silence returned.

His entire life's experience was bound to that fragile soil, to the sound of those crickets now penetrating my mind, tolling my mounting drowsiness.

After two bottles were finished, he finally spoke again.

"Do you like it there?"

I was already drowsy, a bit foolish. It took me a moment to remember what he was referring to.

"Yes, I like it a lot. Ever since I went there to study, I never wanted to leave. You'd like it too. But maybe you'd prefer somewhere more rural..."

"These grapes right here — I prune them at the end of September, on the full moon. You educated folks may think it makes no sense, but it changes everything. Out there, there'll be no winters. There'll be no phases of the moon."

I did not doubt this had once been important, but thinking about seasons on present-day Earth was absurd. Yet I did not want to lecture on Meteorological Engineering or parade the scientific and promotional advances on the subject. I knew I could not argue. I owed him more than that.

The truth was that Earth's climate had been lost and the natural mechanisms of ecological balance exhausted. The planet's maintenance depended on complex, fragile machinery — and a great, great deal of money.

"You're right, Grandpa." I needed to be careful. "But the ecological maintenance of Earth is unsustainable. It's far easier to keep the air clean and water drinkable in smaller cities built specifically for that purpose than to adapt a planet this large. Most of my generation has never even set foot on Earth. The current leaders see Earth as nothing more than a money pit — a very expensive museum to maintain, one that could go up in flames at any moment."

"You're telling me a bulldozer's coming to rip up the soil? To destroy my vineyard — our family's vineyard for centuries — and haul what's left to some space city? Just because a few rich businessmen and the government think this place no longer... turns a profit?"

"Yes, Grandpa. That's exactly it." I replied, melancholy. I understood his rage at my participation, at my compliance. But the destruction of our planet had begun long before me. I felt responsible only for doing the best I could for those who stayed and for my children up above. I continued.

"It would be better if you left, through the Migration Program. I don't say that just because I work for it. You know — before they shut down all the controls on this place. They say they'll wait another generation, but who can guarantee it? At any moment they could decide to shut down the machines... no rain, no water — how would you survive? I'll talk to the people at the Restructuring Agency about setting aside this soil — from your farm — specifically for you. I'll personally oversee the transfer. You can teach your grandchildren to plant. How about that? It'll be the same soil... just in a new place."

He knew he had no choice. He knew it was better to go with me and my lime-green vest, with the Migration Program's huge logo. It would be worse if someone showed up in uniform, holding a gun in one hand and an eviction order in the other.

Two weeks later, we left. With great effort, I managed to cut through the bureaucracy and reserve a portion of the soil he had once owned. I made a point of being there as they removed each container of earth, and I was present when they loaded them onto the enormous space docks.

***

The entire relocation — his and that of many other holdouts — kept me busy. It took nearly two years before I could visit him with my family in his new home. A small plot of roughly six hundred square meters on an agricultural satellite, where humanity's large-scale farming was now concentrated.

At the back of the small lot, I noticed several grapevines, occupying a third of the total area.

My children laughed and delighted in the sweets, making faces as they drank the strong concentrated grape juice. It was June, and my daughter managed to sneak a cup of quentão — I pretended not to see.

They were amazed, but I could not share their wonder. I tasted something different from what they tasted. I knew the grapes would never again be as smooth and soft as I remembered. Everything felt like an imitation of what could have been. How much of that was my fault?

I felt small and speechless in the midst of that sketch of a landscape, touched by the coldness of fate, unnerved by the smallness that man had created.
