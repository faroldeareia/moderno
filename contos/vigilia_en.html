Vigil

JULY 21, 2021 • SCIENCE FICTION

Our house balanced atop one of the hills that rose like ancient vertebrae — dorsal spines of a world asleep in stone.

From an early age I learned it was no accident that we lived so high, touching the clouds. The importance of our lives was to remain watchful, on guard for something I had not yet discovered.

From where we stood, shielded by escarpments, we could see the entire valley: the thin trees like fingers filling the void left by the meanders of a river; the birds, the clouds, the suns and the moons.

That is how I would describe my world — my gaze captured by the movement of beautiful things. My parents would probably speak to you solely of what they called "paths": the empty gaps that determine the routes between any point of origin, distant, and our house. They were the vacant, monotonous stretches of sky or the faded trails between the mountains, like scars.

So there we were, the three of us: sitting in silence, faces turned in every direction, like sentinels of the invisible — now toward the sky, now toward the trails.

Sometimes my father descended the trails, taking our animal with him — a creature with a sturdy back, half donkey, half legend. When he returned, he brought strange relics from the world beyond ours: nails, rope, meat.

Were there children beyond the trail? I don't know. I was rarely allowed to go as far as the stream, let alone that far. Mother explained that there was nothing out there worth seeing. Only our vigil, from the top of our hill, was worthwhile.

On those calm and routine days, my company was modest, composed solely of some "chicken-like" birds — clumsy creatures, but loyal. They were not real chickens, nor anything one could find in the forgotten books down below. My parents never tried to properly name them — they simply said they belonged to that undefined fauna, just another curious note in the dissonant symphony that was our world.

But I saw something more in them. Each of the "chicken-likes" was someone to me. Unique individuals, with proper names: Beaky, Colorful, Runaway, and — oh, how I miss her — Sleepyhead. I collected their eggs, and sometimes cooked them.

It was through Sleepyhead's death that I truly learned what sadness was. I remember one monsoon in particular, a truly frightening one. Not that the others weren't. During them, there was the ritual of my parents preparing the house, and then we would sleep piled together with the animals in the cellar, hidden from the cold, the wind, and the thunder. But that night, Sleepyhead did not wake up.

My father said it was the shock of the thunder. I thought it impossible. She was old, wise, familiar with the storms. But sometimes, I thought, even the creatures most accustomed to the tempest grow weary.

My father was like worn stone: gray, wrinkled, unyielding. When I once cut my foot, I spent days whimpering, wanting to be held and fussed over, which my mother found amusing. He, when he suffered an even deeper cut, merely growled in frustration — not at the pain, but at the wasted time and the soiled rags. As though he were too tired even to feel it.

From so much watching, I became an expert in subtleties that shifted fates. By the pattern of a flock above the river, I knew if there were fish — and whether we would eat. By the drowsy drift of the clouds, I could predict rain weeks in advance. A single unusual glimmer in the red of one of the Suns, and I feared fires creeping through the woods.

I loved the contrast of the blue sky against the red of the rocks. In our world, color came from the birds — and from them alone.

Their flight was my entertainment. The ones that fascinated me most were those that never stopped, crossing in the distance, indifferent to our mountain, our house, our vigil.

I once told my mother I wanted to fly. To go to the end of the valley, to touch the stars. She cried. And my father forbade me from saying it again. I wanted to tell them it was only a daydream, that I knew wings would not sprout from me.

And so I learned to speak with them only of practical things. How many eggs I had gathered, the number of corncobs still stored, or my impressions of the weather. Especially the weather.

Once, I remarked that the monsoons were coming. The clouds were already flying low, tinged gray, and at times they unraveled into a cold mist that dripped through the branches like a lament.

My parents went to the cellar to line the walls with dry grass and dig trenches in the ground beneath the stone floor. When the monsoon water reached the floor, we would raise it with more stones and timber. It was always a balance between living for months in a damp environment, sleeping on the mud of a waterlogged floor, versus the risk of staying in the house, exposed to the wind that might carry the walls — and us with them.

That was when I saw it.

Something was coming through the sky.

No — many somethings.

I knew at once: they did not belong to the landscape. They were too strange. They flew straight, without wavering, ignoring the whims of the air currents. Unlike the birds, they were not colorful, but black and reflective, like the dark, polished stone my mother kept in a small box, or like the calm stream on a grave moonless night.

They were also loud. Even from afar, I could hear a faint and growing hum. Everything in our world learned quickly that silence was vital for survival. Our birds did not sing.

So many rules that nature imposed upon our world, which I had intuitively accepted as truth, being broken by that novelty, relentless in its slow approach. Those things were made by men — and men from far away, whom I had never known.

I shouted for my father, shouted for my mother.

My father stood rigid beside me, rested his hand on my shoulder, and sighed. He told me to light the bonfire. My mother began to cry — a different kind of crying.

That was when I understood that our vigil had been nothing but a waiting, and it had at last come to an end.

***

Author's Note: This is a story marked by a perhaps more enigmatic style of writing, with a rhythm of its own. While other stories of mine explore the experience of being on an isolated planet without communication — as in "Hope in Debris" — here I wanted to experiment with that same feeling in a more dreamlike way, also as a writing exercise, perhaps reminiscent of some more contemporary authors.
