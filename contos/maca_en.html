An Apple

MAY 19, 2025 • FICTION • SCI-FI

Every week, Dário returned from the Central with the familiar burden: sealed packages, stacked, labeled in institutional gray: "Ration Type B," "Energy Paste vanilla flavor," "Lipid Supplement — family." Standard fare. Optimized nutrients, approved by the Organization — everything rigorously within expectations.

"Rations and paste again," Alex grumbled, boredom echoing in his voice. "I'd love to try a fruit, at least once in my life."

Dário would sigh in response. Fruits were a prohibitive luxury. Unbelievably expensive. They weren't even sold in Sector Eden 6 anymore, banished to the Upper Zones where few could reach them.

The days dragged on in controlled routine: the hum of automated transports, work in identical luminous cubicles, standardized holographic entertainment, the ever-present rations and paste. Until the day Dário came home with a different energy.

"Alex! You won't believe this!" Dário's eyes shone with a tense excitement. In his hands, an opaque box sealed with a security tab Alex had never seen before.

"What is that?" Alex asked, curious.

"My bonus! I spent most of it," Dário answered. "But... it's for you."

Carefully, Dário opened the box. Inside, nestled in protective material that looked like sheets of aluminum foil, there was... a fruit. An apple. Red and golden, imperfectly round, with a small stem and a faint waxy sheen on its skin. It had a smell... different. Sweet, but with something earthy about it.

"My God. Is this... is this real? Does this actually exist?" Alex gesticulated, barely containing his excitement.

"It does. It costs the equivalent of a year's worth of paste, but it exists," Dário replied.

Alex picked up the apple with both hands, as if it were a precious artifact. Its surface wasn't perfectly smooth; there were small indentations, variations in color. It was strangely heavy.

"How... how do we eat it?" Alex asked suddenly, euphoria giving way to comical bewilderment.

Dário furrowed his brow.

"Ah. Well... we just... bite into it, I think?"

They looked at each other, the realization of their own ignorance dawning on them. Rations and paste came ready to eat. What were the steps for an apple?

"It has some spots. Is that normal?" Alex asked, partly to Dário, partly to himself.

The house AI was consulted.

"Sanitization of fresh fruit: dilute one tablespoon of a two percent sodium hypochlorite solution in one liter of potable water. Recommended immersion time: fifteen minutes," the AI informed them.

"Do we have that stuff?" Dário asked Alex.

The AI itself answered:

"There is no sodium hypochlorite in stock."

"Then we can't eat it." Alex sighed.

Dário laughed.

"Of course we can. People used to eat these all the time, without washing them. Even children ate them, straight off the tree."

Skepticism lingered on Alex's face.

"But... what about these little specks here? Is that fungus?"

"We can peel it," Dário suggested. The fruit had been far too expensive to be discarded so easily.

"Peel it? That sounds like a lot of work. I need a tutorial. And... the apple has this thing right in the middle! Here, this lower part. What if some bug crawled in?"

"That's where the core is, maybe." Dário didn't seem certain either.

With difficulty, they tried to cut a slice. The standard knife, bladeless, didn't glide smoothly; the fruit's inner texture offered resistance in an unexpected way. When they finally managed a piece, the cut surface began to change color slightly, turning opaque.

Alex went pale.

"I... really wanted this, you know? But now it seems like the fruit is trying to kill me."

He pushed the apple back into the box, frustration plain in the gesture.

"Hand me a vanilla-flavored paste, please."

***

Note: This is a short tale, with a bit more humor. It was born from the fact that my wife and I have different views on what constitutes an edible fruit. She tends to be quite concerned about blemishes on strawberries, which I (forgive me for saying) find a bit excessive.
