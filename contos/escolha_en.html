How long does a Choice last?

JANUARY 16, 2026 • SCIENCE FICTION • SHORT STORY

Lucas always said their love was a matter of density. He, a geologist, loved the weight of things, the permanence of granite, the certainty of solid ground. She, Helena, a prodigy of propulsion engineering, loved speed, escape, the imponderable.

When Icarus V departed — humanity's great project, her great project — they were both 23 years old.

"We'll talk all the time, the trip is quick," she said at the boarding terminal, her smooth skin reflecting the platform lights. "It'll be almost a year of travel."

"And decades here," Lucas replied, holding her hand.

Icarus V would be the first ship to finally test the sub-real jump at intersolar scale. But even with the perforation of reality, relativity was a non-negotiable rule.

"We have the Mirror." She touched the device on his wrist. "Particle entanglement. Distance doesn't matter, nor does time distortion. We'll talk every day."

He promised. She left.

The problem with "every day" was relativity. For Helena, breakfast on the ship lasted thirty minutes. In that interval, on Earth, months would pass.

In Helena's first video, she complained about the synthetic food. Her hair was pulled back in a loose ponytail, her eyes bright with excitement. Lucas replied. He already had a thin beard and dark circles from studying for his doctorate.

In Helena's twentieth video, she told him about a capacitor failure she had fixed in two hours. Lucas replied holding a wedding invitation. Not his own, but from a mutual friend Helena swore she had seen "yesterday."

"You're going gray," she said, laughing, her image floating above his dinner table. She was still 23. She wore the same t-shirt from launch day.

"It's the stress of being a professor," he lied.

Time passed. Lucas's geology settled into layers. He met Clara at a conference on volcanism in Freya-2. Clara was real, solid, present. She didn't travel at the speed of light; she took the subway and liked hot tea.

They married. They had a daughter. They grew old. But the Mirror was never turned off. Helena became a kind of digital ghost, a timeless friend living in perpetual youth, while Lucas's life ran in time-lapse.

When Helena finally arrived at Proxima B, she was biologically 24 years old. Lucas was 54.

Without Icarus V's relative velocity distorting space-time, the Mirror's technology finally worked in perfect sync.

Lucas was sitting on the porch of his country house. His marriage to Clara was heading toward divorce, or so he thought. His daughter was making plans to study on Mars. The nights were long and silent.

The Mirror beeped. Helena's image appeared. She was radiant, with the red sun of Proxima B illuminating her young, wrinkle-free face.

"We made it, Lu!" she shouted. "Terraforming is about to begin. It's beautiful. You should see this. Maybe even in person?"

Lucas looked at his own hands, age spots beginning to appear on his skin. He looked at her, the young woman around whom his mind had orbited an entire lifetime.

That's when the idea struck. A simple, brutal equation.

He opened his tablet and pulled up the schedule for the next heavy cargo ship heading to the Proxima sector, using the now well-established sub-real jump technology.

His hands trembled over the keyboard. It was mathematically perfect. He would board, sleep in cryostasis while traveling at impossible speeds, frozen in an amber of dilated time. One year for him. Twenty years for Helena.

Finally synchronized. Similar ages, the same marks of time, the same accumulated weariness. They would meet in the future under alien suns.

"Lucas?" Helena called from the hologram. "You're quiet."

He didn't respond right away. His heart beat hard, out of rhythm. All he had to do was go. Leave the empty house, the imperfect marriage, the sedimentary memories, and hurl himself into the void to find the one constant in his life.

"Lu?" she insisted, worried.

He looked at Helena's young face. She had just arrived. Her life on Proxima was only beginning. She was a pioneer, full of energy, with an entire world to build.

"What if..."

The thought was devastating. Twenty years was a long time.

"What if I'm no longer the same person she fell in love with?"

"What if she's no longer the same person I fell in love with?"

"What if there's no room for me in her new life?"

Lucas realized that some distances aren't measured in light-years. They're measured in choices. And some choices take us to places from which there is no return.

***

A reflection on relativity — not only physical, but emotional — and how time shapes identities in irreversible ways.
