Jorge Ji-Tae

FEBRUARY 4, 2019 • DRAMA • MARTIAL ARTS

Jorge Ji-Tae could no longer feel his ribs. In their place was only a sharp pain, one that had surpassed the peak a human body could endure. Not even new blows there had any effect. The pain numbed him. He glanced at the clock: 30 seconds. He just needed to hold on for 30 more seconds.

"I need to stay standing," he thought. That was the most important thing. It was all that mattered. Inhale, exhale. Inhale, exhale. He focused on his abdomen rising and falling slowly — it diverted the pain from his back. So he did not notice the imminent kick coming for his face.

"On your feet." 15 more seconds. "Focus, man," he talked to himself.

A kick to the thigh.

A kick to the chest.

"Still standing." And... "Amen" — it was over.

"Jorge Ji-Tae, you threw the fight!" shouted his father, Mr. Jun Ji-Tae — opponent and master. "Pathetic."

"I'm sorry, sir," Jorge answered automatically, collapsing to the floor. He did not have the courage to look at anyone. He forced a cough, searching for an excuse to stop. "Damn — no blood."

Deep down, he would not mind being considered a coward, but he could no longer stand this training routine. Even so close to the final for the national Taekwondo selection. The bout for the sole Olympic spot was near.

He knew his father would give no quarter.

"Pay attention, Jorge. You must focus on your hips. You still counterbalance with your arms every time you use your legs. That throws off your balance."

He wanted to answer: only against you, you damn perfectionist. His father was a three-time international champion, born and trained in Korea itself, with the heart and body of a mountain. He knew he would never be as good, but so much correction was unfair — after all, he had been undefeated for years.

"Don't be arrogant, Jorge Ji-Tae!" his father barked again, as if reading his student's mind.

"Sorry, Father."

"Then start training now. One thousand high kicks, two hundred 540s, and two hundred side kicks! Forget the punches — you need to relax those arms!" the master ordered, picking up a bamboo rod from the wall. "And every time you tense those shoulders, even for a second — ah!"

"Nê!" Jorge affirmed, trying to sound more enthusiastic than he really was. "Here we go again."

***

The bout would take place in a gymnasium in Rio de Janeiro. The Ji-Tae family was not accustomed to that heat, and it seemed that week hell itself had risen to earth. Jorge kept wondering whether his opponent was also finding it this hard to breathe. Or was it not the climate?

He had fought him before, in several other competitions over the past twenty years. Of those, he had not lost in at least fifteen. He hoped his luck would not change today.

His body was worn, marked. Hundreds of reddish lines could be seen on his exposed skin. His opponent showed no such marks. "He didn't train seriously. This'll be easy," Jorge thought.

The fight would be three rounds of three minutes each. Jorge never stopped thinking about the irony of decades of training boiling down to just nine minutes. It was unfair, but his father had taught him well: that's life. No time for pity.

The referee gave the signal. "It's on."

In twenty seconds, nothing happened. In thirty, Jorge had been hit twice — always on counterattacks. By the end of the first round, he took a kick to the chin and nearly blacked out.

"Jorge Ji-Tae, what the hell are you doing?" his father asked as the interval began.

"It's the counterattacks. He always manages to dodge and land one right after."

"It's your balance. I warned you!" his father accused. "If it weren't for that..."

Jun Ji-Tae stepped away and spoke to the referee. Jorge could not make out what they discussed, but he was grateful it would delay the next round.

"Jorge, let's go to the locker room. I told the referee you need to pop your shoulder back in," his father commanded.

It was a lie. His shoulder was visibly fine. But the referee was an old friend and allowed the absence, as long as it was brief.

And now they were alone. Just the two of them and the lockers. And a hammer.

He paid no mind when his father picked up the hammer. He even started to assume a defensive stance when he saw the tool raised. Incredulous, he barely saw the blow. It was too fast and too precise. It struck his shoulder.

And his father struck again. And again. And again. Jorge forgot he knew how to fight; he merely murmured, in vain: "Please, Father, no."

In less than a minute, both his shoulders and arms were shattered.

"There, Jorge Ji-Tae — you'll never tense those shoulders again! Now go fight!"
