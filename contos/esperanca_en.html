Hope in Debris

DECEMBER 29, 2021 • SCIENCE FICTION

My twelfth birthday fell on the same day as the Festival of the Fall. Despite the festive name, it was not a happy occasion. It was an almost funereal moment: the adults would gather, light lanterns, open bottles of liqueur, and sing songs about distant worlds.

My grandmother used to cry.

That is why I do not like receiving presents on my birthday. Even today, it simply feels wrong. But on that particular day, I did. My father finally announced he would take me to the surface.

My mother was furious, argued. I heard her reel off her long list of dangers: "uncatalogued venomous animals; falling radioactive and incandescent debris; acid rain, sandstorms, and unnameable meteorological phenomena; and the certain and ultimate possibility of getting lost and dying of hunger or cold."

She always closed this list, which I had long memorized, with the phrase: "we do not belong and we are not welcome here."

But my father simply smiled and, as always, she relented. His smile had the power to settle any argument.

I packed my own backpack. I set aside the lantern, some hard biscuits baked by my grandmother, and my thermal coat. I did not forget the most important thing: my amulet.

I had received it on another birthday. My father had said it was the most important part of a ship. Venturing out to the wreckage of the Ship was a great risk, so that piece must truly have been special, for I remember the danger he faced to find it. I did ask its name once; I recall his excitement as he explained it, but I soon forgot. It is easy to forget what you do not understand, especially in childhood.

I never asked again what that object really was. My father was respected by everyone as an engineer, repairing and assembling vital equipment. I did not want them to think me a daughter with no aptitude for mechanics, someone incapable of remembering names, tarnishing some hereditary honor.

So, to me, it was simply an amulet.

With supplies ready, we departed from the vault we call home, in the heart of the cavern that shelters our civilization. We followed a long, narrow tunnel where minerals in the walls shifted colors as we passed — my father radiated a calm blue, I a vivid orange. The glow was reinforced by cold, opaque lamps symmetrically installed, but the path, once smooth from constant use, was now seldom traveled, erosion slowly reclaiming its dominion and dust settling beneath our feet.

I should say that, despite his apparent age, my father was agile, crouching or scaling natural steps with ease. Perhaps he was not so old after all; perhaps today I am older than he was then. We continued to thread our way through the corridors until a strange light — pale, at first timid and diffuse — bathed our path.

I knew what it was; everyone did, even those who had never seen the surface. The light of the star this planet orbited, which we had never bothered to name. Not out of ingratitude — it was our greatest source of energy. But for our ancient society, stranded on an anonymous planet, nature was viewed with impersonal pragmatism. Surely, somewhere else, that star had a name composed of letters and numbers, as in a catalog.

Near the opening from which the light radiated, it intensified, making my eyes sting and water. My father, with his constant smile, noticed promptly and handed me the protective mask.

It was a simple visor of darkened glass held by an elastic band. As I slipped it over my head and adjusted it over my eyes, the elastic snagged a tuft of my hair, provoking a sharp sting. I straightened my shoulders instantly, blinking away tears of pain. This was no time to show weakness.

He nodded — perhaps satisfied with my readiness, or simply unaware of the small incident — and pointed toward the path ahead.

"The climb is just past that bend, through a shaft. The light will be even more merciless up there. Let your eyes adjust gradually, all right?"

It was not long before the path, until then horizontal, became abruptly vertical. Hooks on the walls formed steps, alongside a series of ropes and a small lifting platform. My father told me to ride it; I refused.

"No way!" I protested, my voice coming out more determined than even I expected. "I'm climbing the steps. I don't need help."

He studied me for a moment, an amused glint in his eyes.

"Stubborn. All right, the steps it is. But on one condition. I'm tying this rope to you." He raised the rope and, before I could even think of arguing against being tied up, he leaned in and added in a conspiratorial whisper:

"And not a word to your mother about this vertical adventure of ours, agreed? Our secret."

I felt a thrill of excitement. A secret! The magic word. Suddenly, the rope was no longer a symbol of dependence but the bond of a secret pact with my father. We climbed — I clumsily, he with astonishing skill.

The first sight of the surface stole my breath — whether from the vastness or the exertion of the climb, I could not say. Everything seemed... impossible. No image or description had prepared me for those notions of distance, of amplitude, for the absence of ceiling or walls, for the sight of the sky.

How could one understand the true meaning of freedom without ever having seen the immensity of the world?

I immediately understood my father's daily absences, the ones that had caused me such jealousy. It was impossible to compete with that.

Perhaps you already know what the surface of some world looks like, but not mine. It is flat as far as the eye can see. Rocks of varying sizes dotted the landscape, some as colossal as our main dwelling in the caverns. Even with them, the prevailing impression was one of immense, empty flatness. There was no sign of plants, no glint of water. The native life, like us, seemed to prefer the safety of the underground.

Nor did there seem to be any dangerous animals. I was certain my mother had been exaggerating.

My father broke my reverie with the expected question:

"So — what do you think?"

Curiously, it was not the open world that most amazed me, but the feeling of having my father there, with me. As if only the two of us existed, in a world created exclusively for that moment of ours.

Underground, it was nearly impossible to be alone. A constant sensation of suffocation accompanied us. I had always known this without understanding why. Our vault sheltered a constant flow of hundreds of people going about their lives. And the echoes... Echoes of generators, greenhouses, exhaust fans. I wanted to savor that moment: me, him, and the sky.

"Is the sky always this gray?" I asked.

"Almost every time I come up it's overcast. Clouds that block the view. But not always. Sometimes, with clear skies, you'll see the debris — thousands of points covering everything. So many they form a second sky. Layers upon layers, like an onion, wrapping around the planet. Somehow, the few gaps in that cloud of space junk and their mirrored surfaces allow this scant sunlight through. With a great deal of luck, you might see a star or two at night."

I had always been curious about the dreaded debris that, among other things, prevented us from living on the surface. Generally, they were inert — floating rubble accumulated over millennia, hovering around the planet. Occasionally, however, they fell in profusion, like a rain of incandescent metal.

From the caverns, we heard nothing.

Some debris appeared natural: rocky fragments of an ancient moon, once immense, now disintegrated. Many others, however, were artificial — the work of some species that had once inhabited the planet or the moon itself.

We did not know who had built that equipment, nor for what purpose. Nothing about the people who had destroyed their own moon. If any trace of civilization remained, it was distant, unreachable. It was ironic to think that the only memories of a people were their garbage. I feel a deep pity for them.

We arrived here, on this planet, by mere chance.

"We were a people of the stars," my grandmother would sigh, her eyes fixed on a past only she could see. "Born and raised on ships that were miniature universes, self-sufficient giants in perpetual motion." So sing the ballads of the Festival of the Fall: nomadic generations, stopping only to mine what was needed and to discard what was not.

A united fleet, powerful — a chain of strength, they say. Until the chain broke.

Internal conflicts caused old and new rifts to surface. What remained was the fracture: bonds undone, sister ships becoming distant specters. And in the wake of that diaspora, adrift, was the ship that carried my grandparents and my parents — the latter, mere children swept up in the impending tragedy.

The elders? They carry that memory like a brand upon the soul, silenced by regret or entrenched in ancestral stubbornness. For the solitude of space does not forgive: isolated, like a branch torn from the tree, they did not know how to heal the wounds of their own mother ship. The story, repeated year after year at the Festival of the Fall until it became almost a myth, tells of a treacherous failure, a mechanical heart that choked and silenced the ship.

Nearly everything aboard — the routine, the life — fed on disciplined stores of solar light. But to tear through the veil between the stars, to leap chasms of vacuum, the brute power of nuclear fusion was needed, dependent on a mineral so rare across the entire universe. And it was there, in the core of the warp engines, that the seed of catastrophe germinated, spreading failures through every vital system. The punishment for arrogance, the most cynical whisper in the shadows of the Festival. Our ancestors ran out of fuel.

As their sole, desperate alternative, their eyes turned to this planet. Perhaps a premonition, perhaps the delirium of despair, but in the rings of debris that imprison us today, they thought they saw the ghost of the rare mineral. And they wagered everything. Who knows — in that junk abandoned by another civilization, they might find the spark to reignite their warp engines.

That happened exactly sixty-two years before my twelfth birthday — the Day of the Fall.

We give the event that name because it was not, in fact, a landing, but a vertiginous and uncontrolled descent.

The debris that crowns this planet, unstable in material and behavior, tore through the ship's innards, robbing it of its last breath of autonomy. Pieces of it, shards of our history, fused with that orbital graveyard.

It was for those debris — the ones that occasionally fell — that my father climbed to the surface. And that was why I climbed with him that day.

The sky, until then merely gray and vast, tore open in a sudden, dark fury, hurling grains of sand that lashed the skin.

My father acted with the swiftness of one who knows the planet's moods. He pulled the protective hood over my head, fastening it firmly over the eye mask. I was encased, but the world outside roared. I thought we would retreat immediately to the safety of the shaft. But he leaned in, his mouth pressed to my ear so that his voice might overcome the howl of the sandstorm.

"DO YOU SEE ANY CRATER? ANY SHIP WRECKAGE?"

No, I didn't. I shook my head.

"EXACTLY!" he shouted, the sound muffled by the wind and the hood. "THESE STORMS LEVEL EVERYTHING! WHAT WAS LEFT OF THE MOTHER SHIP HAS LONG BEEN LOST BENEATH THESE SANDS! LOOK OVER THERE!"

Even with limited vision, I followed his gloved finger. He was pointing to an almost imperceptible ripple in the wind-swept terrain, a subtle mound I would never have noticed on my own.

He continued:

"THE WIND ALSO HIDES THE IMPACT CRATERS!" His voice was laden with an urgency that made me hold my breath. "THEY BECOME QUICKSAND TRAPS! IT WAS IN ONE OF THOSE THAT YOUR UNCLE—" He hesitated. "DIED. WE COULDN'T PULL HIM OUT IN TIME, LIKE SO MANY OTHERS."

I nodded.

"AND AT THE BOTTOM OF THOSE CRATERS, RAINWATER COLLECTS. IT PERCOLATES, FILTERED, DOWN TO US. BUT WHAT SETTLES AT THE BOTTOM OF THE CRATER ATTRACTS ANIMALS. SMALL, BUT LETHAL."

Then I understood my mother's warning. The dangerous animals were precisely the smallest, the hidden ones.

Suddenly, I wanted to ask my father. What happened to my uncle? Was he buried, or caught by animals?

The storm devoured all my senses. The wind-borne grains of sand had become small pebbles that drummed painfully against my body. I hunched over, driving my feet down with all my strength to keep from being swept away. Every second there was a small eternity of effort. My father's habitual smile had vanished entirely, replaced by a solemn gravity.

He gripped my arm with a force that was both anchor and determination, and then, as if about to entrust me with the most vital secret in the universe, his voice — still shouted — shifted in tone:

"YOU NEED TO UNDERSTAND THE CRATERS, DAUGHTER!" His eyes sought mine through the visors. "DO YOU KNOW WHY? THEY ARE MORE THAN DANGER!"

He paused. I knew, with the absolute certainty of a child, that what was coming was crucial.

"THEY FORM WHEN THE DEBRIS FALLS! AND THAT DEBRIS... THOSE FRAGMENTS FROM THE SKY... ARE OUR ONLY HOPE!"

My father spaced his sentences, so I could absorb the weight of the message.

"THE ANCIENT TECHNOLOGY THEY CARRY, THE EXOTIC MATERIALS... WE CAN REPURPOSE THEM! BY SOME MIRACULOUS MEANS, THEY SURVIVE THE FALL! OUR MISSION — MINE AND NOW YOURS — IS TO FIND THEM BEFORE THE SAND BURIES THEM FOREVER!"

I could still feel his firm hand gripping me to the surface. His gaze was fixed.

"ONE DAY, I KNOW, ONE OF THESE ARTIFICIAL METEORS WILL BRING THE NUCLEAR ISOTOPE WE NEED! NOT FOR THE SHIP... THAT DREAM DIED WITH THE FALL! BUT FOR SOMETHING SMALLER, INFINITELY MORE IMPORTANT! A COMMUNICATOR!"

"Communicator." The word vibrated in my mind, sharper than the roar of the storm. Like a bell long silenced, wanting to ring.

"A SIGNAL!" My father's voice was almost a roar of faith against the universe, his eyes blazing with a feverish intensity. "A CRY ACROSS THE VOID! TO SEND A MESSAGE AT WARP SPEED! A BEACON IN THE DARK IMMENSITY, ANNOUNCING TO THE UNIVERSE: WE ARE HERE! WE ARE STILL ALIVE!"

"Communicator." The name... I knew it. The memory struck me like lightning: my amulet. The piece that my father, on another birthday, had called "the most important part of a ship." Not just any part. It was that. A beacon, dormant around my neck for so many years, waiting only for a spark to awaken and fulfill its purpose.

I answered with all the conviction a twelve-year-old girl — heir to a legacy of stars and dust — could muster. My voice would have been useless against the storm, but he read the promise in my eyes. I gripped my amulet.

"We're going to turn on this communicator. Tomorrow, or on one of the many tomorrows to come. We're going to turn it on."

***

Author's Note: This is a story I hold dear, perhaps because of the delicate relationship between father and daughter — a theme that, as a father myself, resonates with me and appears in other stories I write. There was no specific moment of inspiration; it was born from the world-building itself: a planet with an unstable surface, where a crew becomes trapped underground. Gradually, that crew transforms into a civilization, and it was from that emergence that the story blossomed, almost on its own. It is a story I must have revised quite a few times (current version revised on May 19, 2025).
