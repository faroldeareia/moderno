Bean Plantation

FEBRUARY 23, 2023 • SCIENCE FICTION

When old Humberto saw the orange-and-blue Civil Defense pickup truck pulling up, he thought it strange. There was no frost or drought that would warrant a visit. He stood there, shovel in hand, watching the agent climb out of the vehicle and walk over, all serious, sweating inside his orange vest.

"Good afternoon, Mr. Humberto," the young man greeted.

"Afternoon, son. Can I help you?"

The agent stared at Humberto for a while, his expression closed. He let out a sigh and got straight to the point:

"You'll have to plant beans from now on." He pulled an official letterhead document from a folder, as proof.

Humberto stared at the paper with a furrowed brow. He didn't budge. He wouldn't have been able to read it anyway.

Instead, he decided to be equally direct:

"The government is taking my land, is that it?"

The young man took a step back. This was the sixth property he'd visited. He thought himself lucky he hadn't been shot yet.

"That's not it. It's because of the atomic bombs. The government is making some adjustments regarding what gets planted. Prioritizing food, ensuring stockpiles, that sort of thing."

Humberto had seen something in the newspaper about a foreign war, nuclear bombs, distant chaos. He just couldn't understand what other people's wars had to do with him or his vegetables.

"Is the government going to pay for the change?" the old man asked, somewhat incredulous.

"They will. An agricultural extension agent from the city will come sort it out." The agent pulled a device from his vest. "I just need to check something first."

The device made a strange noise, like a little radio out of tune. Humberto held back his curiosity — he didn't care for these city gadgets.

The agent explained anyway what it was, following established protocol.

"This is equipment for measuring contamination. Don't worry, it won't affect your crops at all!"

"You'll have to excuse me, but there's no contamination here. I don't even use pesticides," Humberto said, looking offended.

The young man sighed.

"It's not for pesticides — it's for measuring radiation. It can travel through the air, through rain, even from far away, you know?" He paused, jotting something down on his form. "All clear. I'll come by monthly to check on you and the plantation."

"Just one more thing," the old man said, wearily. "It's fine to pull up my cabbages, tomatoes... But I'd rather not touch the coffee plants."

"Fair enough. We'll always need coffee. Take care, sir."

Humberto murmured a goodbye.

As the pickup truck passed through the gate, he decided that on the next visit, he'd offer the agent a cup of coffee. You know, as a thank-you.

***

With every visit, the agent ran the device over the small plantation, came inside, and drank two cups of bitter coffee.

It was on the tenth visit that the device finally made a strange noise, beeping like a nervous kettle. Humberto didn't need to be an expert to interpret a bad sign.

"What's that?" the old man asked. "Is the soil contaminated?"

The young man scratched his head and nodded.

"Even the coffee?" Humberto pondered. "I'll throw the pot out."

The young man thought for a moment. He remembered how hard things had gotten and how much harder they would become.

He shrugged.

"Nah, no need. Let's go finish it."

After downing the customary second cup, the agent had only one thought: at least the taste was still good.

***

Author's Note: This is a short piece, with more humor than usual for my writing. I was a bit short on topics, and, working at Civil Defense, I thought about mixing that with science fiction. That's how both this story and its sibling, "Meteor-lit Dinner," came to be.
