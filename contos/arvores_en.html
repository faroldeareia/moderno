Fruitless Trees

JANUARY 19, 2024 • SCIENCE FICTION • SHORT STORY

Demétrio returned to what remained of his childhood home, a ruin of twisted metal and crumbling bricks, reclaimed by a forest of flowering cacti.

Few people remained in the colony by that time. The minerals had been depleted, and the remaining inhabitants lived off the extraction of cactus oil, shipped as exotic raw material on space freighters.

It was better this way, he thought. There would be no one to dig up his old words and make mockery of his return. After all, who was the self-important one who thought the heat was unbearable, the lack of entertainment mind-numbing, the neighbors devoid of imagination?

Now the colony suited him well. During the rainy summer, he could stay home and paint. Not with digital brushes, but with real ones. During the parched winter, he could visit the abandoned mines, flooded with fine fish.

But his childhood home was beyond repair. That was why he had entered the house recommended by the young real estate agent. He assessed the modest rooms, easy to clean, and the spacious yard. There was even an orchard!

The dilemma was that Demétrio knew this house, and for him, it was a place marked by melancholy.

"The Lopes family used to live here," he said, partly to himself, partly to the agent.

The agent shrugged. To her, that surname meant nothing.

Demétrio's family, like so many others in the colonies of this and countless other planets, belonged to the Sacred Order of Expansion. His mother, tireless, always warned him to keep his distance from the Lopes, and the mere mention of that surname would make her involuntarily cross her hands in a religious gesture of protection.

Demétrio took his mother's warnings seriously and avoided befriending Laura Lopes, who was roughly the same age. Nor her older brother, who was responsible for walking the colony's streets, collecting garbage and taking it to the recycling station.

The truth was that Demétrio saw nothing different about the Lopes family. The colony was populated by people from every corner, of every kind.

"Because they are Spacers," his mother declared, when once pressed, as though that brief explanation were sufficient.

"All right. And what does it mean to be a Spacer?"

"Well... A long time ago, some people left our home planet to live only on ships, without soil to sow," his mother answered. Then, in a monotone, she recited: "Build houses and dwell in them; plant gardens and eat of their produce."

"But the Lopes live here, not on ships. They even have an orchard!" he insisted.

"Son, a fruit is no better than the tree that bore it. Now, enough questions — go take your bath," his mother scolded.

Demétrio hated it when adults spoke in parables. He had many other questions about the Spacers, but also about why the cacti glowed, how the three moons avoided colliding. He knew that neither his mother, nor anyone else in the colony, could answer them.

"That's why I left," Demétrio recalled, "in search of answers."

The agent called his attention back, explaining how the house had been modernized: plumbing, air conditioning, fresh paint. Everything brand new.

"Can you tell me what happened to the previous owners?" he asked the agent, ignoring the list of improvements.

"Um. One moment," the young woman replied, checking the tablet in her hand. "The listing doesn't usually have many details. Well, the current owner is Laura Lopes, and the place of residence is listed, hmm, as Colony Eden."

Demétrio smiled, gazing at the house, still lost in thought.

"So, are you interested?" the agent asked, restless.

A scent of lemons drifted through the damp air, emanating from the orchard. His mother's words echoed in his mind, speaking of seeds, gardens, and fruit. Yet he wondered whether she had ever tasted anything from Earth.

It was a singular fragrance, and, although Demétrio had traveled to so many places, across so many colonies, lemons were no longer common. Soil by soil, planet by planet, the fruit had become something entirely different.

Demétrio's mind turned once more to the orchard before him. He imagined whether the seedlings had been brought directly from Earth, planted in a pot, nourished by recycled water and artificial light aboard a spaceship, carefully tended by generations of Lopes.

As he breathed in deeply the citrus aroma, he made his decision with conviction.

"Yes, I'll take the house."

Note: Originally submitted on 01/19/2024 and re-edited on 01/22/2024. This tale explores the idea of roots and traditions in a future where Earth is a distant memory, and how small fragments of it (such as the scent of a lemon) can carry immense emotional weight.
