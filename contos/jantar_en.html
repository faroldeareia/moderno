Meteor-lit Dinner

FEBRUARY 22, 2022 • SCIENCE FICTION

Old Ademir was in the backyard, cracking open his first beer can, when he called Rosa, his wife, to come see the comet that would destroy the Earth.

Rosa shouted from inside the house that she was coming, she just needed to find her slippers.

"Come barefoot, woman. You're going to worry about dirtying the house today of all days?" the husband grumbled.

"It's not that — it's the ants. The yard is crawling with anthills, and you still haven't done anything about it."

Ademir stared at the ground, and soon his eyes adjusted to the many little ants marching back and forth, oblivious to the smoldering ball in the sky.

Finally, Rosa appeared, wearing a pair of fancy little shoes she had worn to at least three weddings that week. Everyone wanted to get married in a hurry.

She sat down at the small white cast-aluminum table, restless.

"My outfit doesn't match the shoes," she exclaimed to the wind, "I'm going to change."

Old Ademir pretended he hadn't heard, but he knew that as soon as she came back in a different outfit, he would have to change to match her.

And he was right. She had set aside a plaid suit for him.

At last, there they were, the two old folks at the little table, with beer and snacks, lit by a red glow from the sky, lovelier than any candle.

Rosa stood up, saying:

"I can't believe we're going to sit here eating this junk food, all dressed up like this, especially today. I'm making dinner." Since old Ademir hadn't moved a muscle, she had to remind him: "And you're coming to help."

Ademir stared into the refrigerator. There wasn't much on the shelves. It wasn't safe to go to the market — if any were still standing after the looting.

His gaze settled on the various bundles of cake, wedding favors, brigadeiros — souvenirs from the recent celebrations. His mouth watered; at least there would be plenty of dessert.

Among all those sweets, he found some cheese, good enough for a sauce. He remembered there was pasta in the cupboard. It would have to do.

Rosa opened a bottle of wine.

Ademir set the plates.

Rosa glanced at the TV, where a man in an orange vest was talking about shelters, basements, cellars. To her, none of that mattered anymore.

She turned off the screen. She put on some music.

Dinner turned out really good, despite the improvisation. Savored slowly, with a taste of memories and laughter.

***

Author's Note: This is a short piece, with more humor than usual for my writing. I was a bit short on topics, and, working at Civil Defense, I thought about mixing that with science fiction. That's how both this story and its sibling, "Bean Plantation," came to be.
