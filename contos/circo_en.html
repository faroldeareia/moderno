The Incredible "Blue Magician" Circus

FEBRUARY 7, 2019 • REGIONALIST • HORROR

The Incredible "Blue Magician" Circus was truly incredible. Well, at least for those people, punished by drought and misery, hardened like the cacti that surrounded them. These sertanejos craved anything that might break the tedium.

And so the children chased the procession of wagons carrying enormous cages draped in black cloth, disassembled tents, provisions, and strange folk of every kind. The young men nudged their sweethearts, inviting them for the evening. The fathers and mothers worried about the danger these performers posed to their unmarried sons and daughters.

But certainly, everyone was surprised that a circus so large had stopped in such a lost and isolated place. Not even the train passed through those parts anymore, the brush having swallowed the old railway. Deep down, everyone assumed they were just passing through. But no — there were dozens of men, on the open ground beside the Main Church, raising three great tents: one blue, one red, one yellow; plus the encampment with dormitories and a mess hall.

News of the circus spread to every farm and hamlet in the region, and by sunset the local population had doubled, swollen with the curious eager for the spectacles they would soon witness.

The Magician, owner of the circus, did not much care for the title "circus." He preferred "House of Spectacles"; he felt "circus" evoked only clowns and jugglers. What he offered was more, much more: in the blue tent, elastic contortionists brought from China; fire-swallowers with fearsome features from the Sahara; fairy-like dancers trained in Russia. In the yellow tent, there was his own grand magic act — real magic, not cheap illusions with mirrors and hollow boxes. And finally, there was the red tent, with its enormous sign bearing two lines: "Only for the strong" and "House of Aberrations."

Any young man felt particularly challenged by that sign. They all thought themselves brave enough. It was no different with the Colonel, who had come initially intending to drive that band of itinerant outcasts from his lands — but not without first, of course, indulging in the performances.

He sat in the best seat, specially offered by the Magician after an unusual visit from the local deputy and some of the Colonel's own men. Beside him sat his young wife, and on the other side, his captain jagunço — his trusted enforcer.

The Colonel found the aberrations a touch tedious. There was the monkey-boy, hairy and with a tail; the bearded lady with her long ginger moustache; an albino dwarf with lidless eyes. He thought he and his men had left enemies in far more aberrant states, through the delicate work of their long knives. Perhaps he should sell some of them to the Magician, he mused.

"SILENCE!" shouted the monkey-boy. The audience fell absolutely still, cutting short the murmurs and titters. Everyone stopped to watch the two twin sisters entering the ring. They were beautiful — extraordinarily beautiful — with reddish skin, long black hair, caramel eyes, and indigenous features. Every man, and even the women, found themselves instantly smitten.

There was nothing different about them beyond their matchless beauty. Not until the monkey-boy, playing assistant, removed their shoes: there were hands where their feet should be. When the monkey-boy removed their gloves, everyone saw feet where their hands should be.

And with a certain contortionism, with the feet that were hands, they fingered a guitar and a violin. Their voices were angelic. A beautiful melody filled the void of silence. The experience differed for each onlooker: some found it amusing, others appreciated the music, others were simply incredulous. But the Colonel... ah, he was enchanted! Not even the women's deformities could quench the passion burning in him. Quite the opposite: he felt more and more drawn, with a bizarre fixation.

It was with that melody echoing in his mind that he could not sleep. He lay imagining himself with those two twins, in bed with him, using their deformities in every unimaginable and profane way. He was a deviant, reveling in his perverted dreams.

He could not stand it. First thing the next morning, he requested an audience with the Magician. To him, he said in a firm voice: "I want an encounter at my house, with the Indian twins. And discretion."

The Magician thought long before answering. He knew that with this kind of man, there was no dialogue. But he did not see his performers as objects. He himself hated the word "aberrations." He had found the two girls, still children, on the bank of a stream, abandoned by their tribe. They were like daughters to him.

"Of course, my lord. But I still need to put on my show tonight. Could it be in the early hours, right after the closing?" the Magician said sadly, receiving the Colonel's agreement shortly after.

On another night, the show took place. The Colonel watched again, unable to concentrate. He was sunk in his lunatic lust, thinking of those inverted feet and hands groping his body. At the end of the performance, before heading off to the awaited night, he reminded the Magician of their deal.

As soon as the night emptied and the village lanes fell deserted, the Magician ordered all his workers: "Take everything down, silently. We leave on the midnight train." No one asked questions; no one reminded him the railway had been abandoned. The Magician, after all, was no ordinary illusionist. They did exactly as he ordered, and a few hours later, they were all boarding the locomotive that appeared.

It was at three in the morning that the impatient Colonel sent the jagunço to find the Magician and fetch the two women at once. At four, he screamed in fury that he would kill the cheating magician. He ordered the jagunço to track down the circus's whereabouts and reminded him that if he did not find the Indian women, he would tie his own daughter's hands to her feet.

And so the jagunço and his gang combed every village and dwelling in the surroundings. He could not understand how an entire circus could have fled the region without anyone seeing. He used threats, money, and guns. He tortured old men and children — all in vain. He imagined the Devil himself was playing a trick on him.

He was already desperate when he decided to find two girls who bore at least a passing resemblance to indigenous features. He kidnapped them and used his skills with blade and needle to swap the girls' hands and feet, while they screamed and cried in agony until they fainted. When they woke, they looked as the Colonel would approve, though with little movement. It'll have to do, he thought.

The Colonel accepted them in silence. He did what he wished for hours. When he sent them away from the room, he called his captain.

"Well then — satisfied?" asked the jagunço, in his rough manner, concealing his anxiety.

"Of course not. They don't know how to sing. But your daughter does, doesn't she?"
