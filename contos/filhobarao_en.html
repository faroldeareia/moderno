The Baron's Son

DECEMBER 13, 2019 • HORROR • SHORT STORY

Warning: Horror story — may contain disturbing passages.

This story was published, with slight adaptations, in Mysterio magazine, issue #5, in June 2021. @revistamysterioretro

The mother pressed the baby against her breasts, ignoring the midwives' stares. It was her child, and no one could stop her from nursing. The midwife and her two apprentices stood silent, their gazes glassy and bodies rigid. The eldest, already white-haired and wrinkle-faced, prayed silently to herself, until at last she managed to murmur, faintly:

"My lady... the baby is not among us. He is with God."

"Of course he's not. Can't you see? My child is feeding!" the mother replied with a smile, gazing tenderly at her son, stroking the back of his bald head.

None of the three midwives knew what to do. It took several — many — minutes, numbed by the sweet lullaby the mother hummed, before the eldest midwife finally spoke aloud, with feigned cheer:

"Well then, shall we call the Baron to see his heir?"

The other two women were immediately relieved by their mistress's cunning. Surely, the man would put an end to this madness.

"Go on, then," the eldest ordered one of the apprentices, after her mistress nodded. "Go on, damn it!"

The Baron was a tall, stout man with a thick white beard. He entered the room brusquely, pipe in his mouth. The smell of rain-dampened wood and the smell of birthing blood mingled with the newly arrived bitter odor of tobacco. He regarded the baby and his wife placidly, without changing any expression on his face. He contemplated the scene for at least three long draws of the pipe. The midwives' anxiety was visible.

On the first draw, the Baron perceived for himself that the baby must be dead. Its already gray mouth did not move, not even pressed against his wife's abundant breast, heavy with milk. Its little head lolled to one side, limp. It did not cry, nor did it seem to breathe.

On the second draw, he determined, from his wife's sweet gaze and delicate gestures, that the trauma was irreversible. He recalled his campaigns in the Paraguayan fields and saw again the madness in the eyes of enemies, incredulous at the massacre they were suffering.

On the third draw, he considered what could be done. His wife was young; she could bear other children — to him, this was obvious. Just another child. But he knew his wife, and he thought her weak. He could not appeal to reason. He found a solution.

"Let us leave my wife alone with my son," the man ordered, without averting his gaze from the scene.

All three immediately obeyed. Their work was done, they thought. Once out of the room, they would bear no further responsibility.

Just outside the door, the Baron fixed the eldest midwife with his stare.

"Where art thou from?" he asked.

"From Toucan Village, sir," the woman answered sheepishly, fearing she would be blamed for the unfortunate birth.

"And are there newborn boys in this village?"

The midwife's heart raced. She feared where this conversation might lead. But lying to her master would be worse.

"Yes. There are. I myself delivered two, this month."

"Good. Go to the Coachman — he is a man I trust. Explain the situation, and do what must be done. Take the healthiest one, the one most resembling my dead son. The Coachman will bring coins and will be armed. It is thy responsibility — dost thou understand the situation?"

"Yes, my lord Baron."

***

An unexpected storm began to lash the estate. The road from there to Toucan Village was not easy, not even on dry days under the Sun. It took the entire night for the midwife to return to the manor.

The woman was drenched, her boots caked with mud. She knew she would dirty the house, but the situation was more urgent than such foolish concerns. She had entered the hall with the Coachman and another absurdly old man — but no baby.

The warmth from the enormous fireplace was comforting, with its calm red light, the crackle of wood, the hot air blowing against their faces. Standing in the center of the hall was the Baron. And he was furious.

"Thou took too long! Where is the baby I asked for? Who is this old man beside thee?"

The midwife answered with self-assurance:

"Sir, forgive the delay. On the way, I thought of a better solution. This old man is known in the village and surroundings as the Blue Shaman."

"A man who lives skulking about in the bush, with false herbs and a thief's tricks," the Baron exclaimed, irritated by the situation.

"No, sir. His magic is real. Let him go upstairs, and you will see." The woman truly seemed certain of what she was saying.

The Baron looked at the Coachman, who nodded calmly:

"I doubted it too, but I was given proof. It could work, master."

"Very well. What is thy price? Is it possible to bring the baby to life? How wilt thou do it?" The Baron asked, staring at the Blue Shaman. He observed that the nickname likely came from a strangely bluish mark, shaped like a rooster, on his neck.

"But of course it is possible. With certain adjustments, anything is possible. He will live long, and he may give you grandchildren. How I will do it does not concern you. But the price — for you — is five hundred coins. The price for your wife shall be her life."

The Baron placed the pipe in his mouth. Grandchildren — that was important. The price in coins was nothing extravagant, a trifle, and he could take another wife in time. On second thought, keeping his own son was a better option than living with a stolen infant of unknown blood.

"Go upstairs now, old man. Tell my wife thou art a physician."

And so he went.

The Baron and the servants heard nothing from the room. Whatever the strange man had said to the woman, or done to her, no one perceived. But all were startled when they heard the cry of a baby, coming from above. Even the midwife, who had placed her faith in the Shaman's magic, seemed surprised.

The Baron did not wait long. He rested his hand on the Coachman's shoulder and whispered:

"We are God-fearing in this house. If it is discovered that I employed black magic and a pact with the Devil, my reputation will be stained. Kill the midwife and the Shaman."

The Baron had barely finished the sentence before the Coachman aimed his pistol at the midwife. With her back turned, she did not even notice the bullet crossing her life.

When the Coachman opened the bedroom door, he saw the Baron's wife — dead. He found the old magician nowhere, yet the bedroom window was still latched from the inside. He had not left through there.

Nor could he have passed through the corridor, as he would have been seen and heard. The Shaman's methods were a mystery.

The Coachman lowered his weapon and observed the baby, still wrapped in the zealous arms of its mother, sprawled cold and pale in an armchair. The baby was nursing, revealing a strangely bluish mark, shaped like a rooster, on its neck.

Author's Note: It's funny — I don't enjoy writing horror stories. There was a time in my adolescence when I read more horror fiction... there were some in the bookshelf at home (in the study), and they were relatively cheap at newspaper stands. But writing it was never a priority. However, on the website Recanto das Letras, there is a horror short story contest, and it's quite a good one. I decided to write this piece for that event. It's a story where I had only the opening in mind, and then the tale unraveled on its own.
