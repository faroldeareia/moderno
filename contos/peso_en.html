The Weight of Silent Souls

MAY 29, 2025 • SCIENCE FICTION • DRAMA

The Research Vessel Professor Ab'Saber glided through the vacuum like a silver needle stitching the dark fabric of the cosmos. The planet Hiisi-X-3 had exhibited anomalous behavior: a ring of debris, uncommonly organized.

"Long-range sensors operational, Captain Rostova." Allura's calm voice — the ship's artificial intelligence — filled the bridge. "No threats detected."

Eva Rostova leaned forward in her chair, eyes fixed on the enormous screen at the front of the bridge.

"Take us closer, Allura. Cautiously."

As the Professor Ab'Saber drew nearer to the planet, the nature of the debris revealed itself. These were no ordinary space rocks. They were... structures. Elongated, with a dull metallic gleam under the faint light of the distant star, translucent in certain spots.

Dr. Aris Thorne, xenobiologist, held his breath. His slender fingers danced across a tablet. "Everyone..." his voice was a murmur laden with astonishment. "There are signatures of organic matter. Inside each of these... objects."

This confirmed the strange readings from the preliminary probes. Debris containing compounds that should not exist in that system and, worse, that radiated heat. An almost unmistakable sign of intelligent life.

Humanity had encountered extraplanetary life before, yes. Microscopic organisms living in tectonic fissures, tiny beings in the steam of colossal volcanoes, algae in methane lakes or in the abysses of deep oceans. This was something of an entirely new magnitude. It was not merely life in an inhospitable environment — it was life defying the laws of nature, with obvious technology. Objects with organic matter inside... it was extraordinary.

"Debris field within high-definition camera range, Captain," Allura reported again.

"Stabilize the ship's velocity and rotation relative to the field. And magnify for us — focus on any one of those objects."

The camera's zoom drew gasps. The debris, which in truth resembled capsules, glowed faintly. Through their translucent sections, a being could be clearly seen: slender forms in a radial body, with five or six long, articulated limbs extending from a central core, reminiscent of elongated, upright starfish. The extremities curled delicately upon themselves, as though embracing an invisible void. The head was merely a smooth protuberance, almost featureless, where tiny points of bioluminescence blinked in an irregular cadence, like the dying glow of a firefly at the end of the night.

"By Allah..." Chief Engineer Farshid's voice broke the silence.

***

The ship's lighting softened, marking the start of the night cycle. Captain Rostova was exhausted, dealing with rumors throughout the vessel and preparing reports for Command. "Well, we are all aware of the weight of our discovery," Eva began, her voice echoing with gravity. "As we speak, I am transmitting all collected data via a jump probe, bound for Command."

She turned to the most gray-haired member of the team. "Dr. Stein, what is your view as an astrophysicist?"

Dr. Stein straightened up. "Captain, it is fascinating! The capsules describe orbits precisely confined to the planet's Roche limit. To put it simply for the laypersons, this is the critical distance for ring-like behavior."

"What I want to know is whether this positioning is intentional..." Eva cut in.

"Deliberate, Captain. Unquestionably. The selection of the planet was not fortuitous. The subtle presence of small shepherd moons ensures the ring's stability. It is an arrangement of remarkable mathematical elegance!"

"But what if we were only seeing the capsules that survived?" asked Sonya, the botanist. "Wouldn't capsules too close to the planet have been swallowed by gravity?"

"A pertinent conjecture! Our projections, however, refute such a scenario. A dispersal event would have resulted in a massive deposition of material on the planetary surface. There would be far more capsules fallen onto the planet than in the ring."

"And those beauties are tough!" Farshid agreed. "They'd survive the fall — many of them."

"Now, the part that worries me," Eva intervened. "Dr. Aris, what do you have on the... passengers?"

Aris spoke softly. "These capsules exist in a kind of symbiosis. The capsule captures solar energy and nourishes the organic tissues, recycling whatever is necessary. But that's not the most astonishing part. The synapses, Captain... are active. But only some of them. The rest of the body... appears dormant."

"What the hell does that mean?" asked Sonya.

Farshid translated: "It means these bodies are being kept alive, conscious. Awake. And just watching. Staring at nothing. By Allah, some of them may be looking at our ship right now!"

"How many of them are in the ring?" asked Sonya.

"Four million, three hundred and seven thousand capsules. All active, except three," Allura answered.

Aris swallowed hard. "This is worse than hell. A body trapped, motionless, adrift... with the brain switched on. Some of these have been here for millennia. If they were human, we would call this torture of the highest order! These capsules should be destroyed. It's an act of mercy!"

Lyra, the xenolinguist, shook her head. "Mercy according to whom, Doctor? What if it was a choice? What if their consciousness doesn't even perceive 'time' the way we do?"

"Everyone, let's stick to the facts... Allura, did the probes on the planet indicate anything?"

"No, ma'am. No planet in this solar system appears to show signs of life. They are completely isolated."

"In a scenario where the variables are indeterminate, which error carries greater moral weight: extinguishing a consciousness that might wish to continue, or keeping one active that might be pleading for cessation?" Dr. Stein finally rejoined the discussion.

Captain Eva Rostova rubbed her face. "Allura. You've monitored all the data. What is your assessment?"

Allura's voice filled the space. "Analyzing the situation, the absolute priority must be observation, with the least possible impact. If I were conscious — if I felt time, if I knew I was alone, motionless — then I would probably prefer to be switched off. It would be an existence of continuous processing, but without purpose."

The bridge fell silent. Captain Eva Rostova remained deep in contemplation. The Professor Ab'Saber continued its vigil, becoming yet another satellite around the mute planet.

***

This tale addresses the existential horror of eternal consciousness without agency, and the moral weight of "First Contact" when it resembles a crime scene or a mausoleum.
