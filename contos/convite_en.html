The Invitation

JANUARY 26, 2019 • DYSTOPIA

The couple was stunned, staring at the screen. They read it again, and again — ten times over, that short message. "Your profile has been selected for the Terramar astro-condominium."

"When did you get your message?" the woman asked.

"Today at eleven. And yours — when did it come?" said the husband.

"Just now, not even ten minutes ago..." she replied, remembering the little icon indicating a new email, blinking cheerfully in the corner of the screen while she watched the news.

At first, she had ignored it. The images of protesters surrounding the news station itself were more eye-catching. It was still the government's first year, but she, as a Social Researcher, knew that these small acts triggered worse things. Those furious people, shouting orders against the very media. Was this how a dictatorship was born?

It had been centuries, since the Sino-MarsAmerican war, since such a high degree of social instability had been seen.

She was a well-regarded scientist in her field, but not the kind the bureaucracy approved of. She had written papers, given interviews and lectures. But the fear of hunger, of thirst, and of water had prevailed for most people. With the majority of the world's nations in collapse, wars and epidemics, any politician promising to seal the borders and purify the souls of his people was easily applauded.

She wept when she saw the foreigners being expelled. She feared for herself, too, when she saw universities and schools being occupied.

She could not blame the people. She disagreed with her husband, a government geologist who believed people would never outgrow the "hunter-gatherer" mentality. He thought humanity was the same as thousands of years ago, but with weapons in place of stones. The brain still trapped in the hunger of the savannas and mystical explanations. Not her. She believed — she needed to believe — that there was something more, a hope, a hidden Civility that would one day resurface.

Her husband worked in a government mineral exploration office. The Universal Mineral Monitoring system — UMM to the technocrats — composed of mobile probes and radio-detection satellites. An Artificial Intelligence mapped every mineral deposit in the known universe and generated economic potential reports. Her husband, thanks to the Human Decision Act — which stated that AIs could not make decisions without human oversight — signed the kilometer-long spreadsheets it generated, putting celestial bodies up for auction to any interested company.

It was monotonous work, but it paid well, and working for the government brought stability amid a time of crisis, unrest, and disarray.

But now, both had received that same message, still there, glowing on the screen: "Your profile has been selected for the Terramar astro-condominium."

It made no sense. She was known to be against the government. Why had she been selected for a colonization? The term "astro-condominium" was just marketing, given the anachronism the word "Colony" carried.

In any case, the invitation seemed like a trap. But was it? She also reflected that the government would not need to go that far to get rid of them — she herself had gone quiet after a while, no longer writing or lecturing; and it was too soon for the government to have the support for a maneuver as blatant as exiling the opposition, whom they called enemies.

"Well, we know the International Housing Agency, which handles these things, is neutral... And the funding comes from the Nordic countries, most of which are still not radical..." her husband reflected aloud.

"Neutral — what do you mean?" She knew neutrality did not exist in humanity. But she just wanted someone to calm her down, even if it meant lying to her, for now.

"Well, we don't know the search parameters. But the UMM probes, for instance, look for rare minerals and cross-reference them with the logistics for their extraction. Think about it: we're both university-educated, thirty years old, we have no children, and we scored top marks on our check-ups. We play sports... To a computer, we're a perfect couple! Plus, I know everything about soils, terrain, and rocks, and you about history, modern anthropology... useful skills on a planet to be explored."

"You I don't understand. You never publish your opinions. But me... any paper I've ever written could be considered subversive in the wrong hands."

He did not know what to say. He had his doubts too. And he knew that not only was the invitation strange, but the mere fact of it being sent felt like a form of threat. A purge or a prize? He tried to calm her.

"I'll check with the people at work, see if anyone knows someone who knows the selection parameters. And find out whether the one signing off on the AI Decision is ours or International."

He didn't discover much. He did learn that the parameters indeed cross-referenced health, education, age, and skills. He also learned that the decision was entirely International, and the signatory country of the Convention on the Use and Exploration of the Universe could not intervene.

He told her this.

"But we still don't know. Someone could be trying to remove people who would be persecuted in the future — a hidden ally — or someone is already persecuting us?"

"That depends. The opportunity is good... The place there looks very nice, already well populated, clean air, a stable society... It shouldn't be bad. We'd have jobs and a reasonable salary."

"But even so — leave our country? Will it be worth it?"

"That depends on how much faith we have in humanity."

***

Author's Note: One of the oldest stories in the archive. An exploration of paranoia in times of social collapse and the irony of being "selected" by an algorithm that ignores politics but not biological and technical potential.
