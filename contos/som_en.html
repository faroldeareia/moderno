The Sound in the Canyon

SEPTEMBER 3, 2021 • SCIENCE FICTION

Tuesday, still four in the morning in the small village of Enchanted Foothill. As on every other week, this was a moment of dread. Punctually, in half an hour, the old mining freighter with the name Ares VI blinking in neon on its hull would pass through the skies. Unfortunately.

Its old turbines, long stripped of their silencers, would shudder frantically while its jets scorched the air. The crooked propellers would whine, tearing through the morning silence. The thunderous vessel would set off the howling of dogs, the crying of babies, and every manner of curse the local population could hurl.

The village of Enchanted Foothill, like every other village along the canyon — touristically billed as "the deepest canyon in the extrasolar colonies," despite there being no tourism whatsoever — lives off the quiet business of artisanal mining, whose exploitation advances from the entrenched valleys to the tops of the planet's steep escarpments, which rise so high they reach what has been conventionally called atmosphere.

And so, the labyrinths of the gorges serve, unfortunately, as the only corridor for all air transport, while also providing a rich acoustics — like a perfect amphitheater for the chaotic roar of that foreign vessel, the Ares VI.

A collective suffering, but one each inhabitant handles in their own way. There are those who plug their ears with rags and pillows; others seek refuge in difficult meditation; and there are those who do not worry about the side effects of sedatives.

The fact was that each villager's timidity made them feel embarrassed to complain or share the torment with their neighbors. It was only two months ago that the issue was truly regarded as a municipal problem.

Old Alastor died after a fall, on a Tuesday morning, at precisely six o'clock. That had been his final dawn trek along the difficult trails that climbed from the valley to the rim of the canyon.

Even though old Alastor suffered from well-known heart problems, labyrinthitis, near-blindness, and a certain alcoholism, it was at his funeral — attended by nearly all five hundred locals — that a small, offhand remark, innocently dropped, raised a larger point:

"The old man had been cheating death for decades," they said. "He'd escaped the jaws of an Ursus, drunk methane, killed five Zeburian pirates with a rock, and all of that on the same day! But in the end it was that damn noisy ship that must have done him in. He died of exhaustion."

Suddenly, amid the nostalgic laughter over Alastor's exploits, came the epiphany. The nuisance of the ship through the canyon was a public health problem. Certainly, even though it had been just a remark, everyone in that moment was sure: yes, he died of exhaustion from being torn from his bed while it was still dark. The guilty party could only be the Ares VI.

The debates began. Someone mentioned that there must be some law, written somewhere in the universe, requiring old ships to use silencers on their turbines. The mayor should enforce it.

"He can't — airspace is controlled by the Star Police," someone observed.

As it turned out, said police were dealing with "bigger problems, such as piracy, smugglers, separatists, and every other class of criminal, leaving no agents to spare," in the words of the Police Agent of the Andromeda Battalion, by official letter.

Realizing they were on their own, they tracked the ship, carefully studied its routes, altitudes, speed. They discovered the ship transported ore from an industrial village belonging to one of those colonial corporations.

They also discovered that the ship was privately owned and part of the fleet of a group composed of decidedly unpleasant people, who now controlled extrasolar transport in the region.

At last, a petition was sent to the mining company.

The reply came in official form, on a document bearing at least ten stamps. It read: "nothing in the contract justifies the termination of the agreement with the vessel Ares VI, as there is no specific clause regarding public disturbance." The messenger and company employee tried to justify himself: "Heavy fees, fines, legal disputes in the case of a contractual termination without just cause, you know how it is."

They then moved on to grander plans: perhaps shoot down the ship, who knows. A silent attack; no warning, no negotiation. Oh, sweet destruction!

"But the falling ship could crash into the escarpments," a teacher pointed out. "And if the escarpment collapses onto the river, burying it? We could create flooding upstream and drought downstream — a total environmental collapse."

That was a problem, they pondered. "And then there's the matter of weapons... We could be mistaken for criminals," another person added. Everyone nodded.

It was delightful to imagine the ship exploding from every angle, in a fireball of freedom, but the quiet local population knew they would never go that far — they were not bad people.

They considered speaking directly with the crew; perhaps it was simply a financial problem for the captain, who could not afford new silencers. They agreed that was not the case — it was impossible for the crew not to notice the disturbance caused by their turbines; it had to be deliberate. Everyone also worried that the crew might hold a grudge against the village. Better for the village to remain unnoticed, to avoid that kind of people. One day, naturally, the ship would be replaced. Perhaps.

Tuesday, four-twenty in the morning. The inhabitants of Enchanted Foothill, their bodies accustomed to the small hours, rise ahead of the noise itself.

They heat the water for coffee, fit the earplugs into the small children's ears, shut the dogs in muffled rooms. In ten minutes, the punctual old Ares VI will pass.

***

Author's Note: This story was born from anger, though it wears the disguise of humor. I've always been an extremely light sleeper. Fortunately, I live in a generally quiet place, which helps. But there was a time — brief, but loud — when a security guard arrived at my building to start his shift on a motorcycle with an open exhaust. He arrived early, always too early. My dog, a German Shepherd of peculiar habits, had a kind of ritual devotion to those motorcycles: at the first sound, he would erupt into long, piercing, terrifying howls. And each howl lingered for minutes... and each minute, at that hour, weighs like hours. There are situations where indignation feels powerless, condemned to the repetition of the nuisance. But one day, I recorded it. I took the recording to the building manager. And, to my surprise, the noise stopped — with courtesy and agreement.
