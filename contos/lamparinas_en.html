Oil Lamps and Gardenias

JANUARY 16, 2024 • FANTASY • SHORT STORY

Fanuskar presses on, weary, the damp breeze breathing against his face.

Sometimes he stares at the rigid clouds in the sky, which descend to earth in a gray mist. The Storm's fall is unpredictable. It is simpler to count the lamps left to light — roughly a hundred to the South Gate, and precisely three thousand five hundred to the East Gate.

His pace is monotonous; the weight of his boot crushing pebbles along the trail that winds beside the colossal wall of lime and stone, from which the oil lamps hang in such symmetry, like flowers of iron and rust.

He cannot say how many he lights each day, but he feels certain he will reach the next gate by nightfall.

His count is regressive, because otherwise, he would not be able to go on.

Each spark from the flint burns his fingers, the friction against the striking stone raises blisters, the glass bulb scratches his skin. There is pus on his feet. The hardest part, however, is preparing the fuel.

The mixture is simple: a measure of his own blood — no other blood would do — mixed with any oil; from nuts, fish, kerosene, it doesn't matter. Over time, Fanuskar learned that draining his blood through a cut on the thigh hurts less.

When at last the glow of the oil lamps surpasses the rays of the setting Sun, he reaches the imposing South Gate. His eyes glide over the ancient poem carved into the portico, words and stanzas long familiar.

This is the Manah Empire, under the blessing of Fire,
Flames that defy wolves in the darkness,
That consume the vile, without hesitation,
And on frigid nights, offer warmth in devotion.
Flames, divine guides, for wayward travelers,
At the borders, eternal torches, lights in communion.
Welcome, where Fire is the guardian.

There was a day he had felt proud reading those stanzas. A young man destined to keep flames that should never be extinguished.

Fanuskar cracks his tired back, scratches the wound on his thigh. "How foolish I was," he murmurs.

***

Everything about Zhi-zi marked her as a foreigner, but it was when she tried to settle the dinner bill with a mere branch of lush leaves that the innkeeper knew for certain.

"Look here, dear, I don't know how it works in your land, but here we use money," the woman said, trying to maintain a serious air, though somewhat embarrassed.

Zhi-zi looked at her sadly.

"I belong to no land."

The innkeeper, hesitant, reached out and took the plant. Part of her felt the need to refuse the payment; another part yielded to curiosity.

Zhi-zi's shoulders relaxed.

"This is Wild Basil. Perfect for seasoning your meals." Her warmth and melodious accent kept the tavern keeper from feeling insulted by the implication.

"All right, then." The innkeeper was about to turn away when Zhi-zi continued:

"This herb came from very far away. It won't take root unless it feels welcomed."

"What?" A pause. The innkeeper noticed Zhi-zi searching for something in her bag.

"Here!" Zhi-zi waved a few scrolls in the air. "These are stories from the lands of this herb. If you read to him now and then, he will feel at home."

The woman's mouth opened, but her words twisted in her throat. Better to change the subject.

"Will you be staying another night?"

"No." Zhi-zi was emphatic.

"Have you looked at the sky lately?" The lines on the innkeeper's face had hardened.

Zhi-zi's gaze crossed beyond the window, where she saw a landscape darkened by a mantle of harsh purple clouds.

"Yes, it looks like it's going to rain heavily." She answered, casually.

"That is no ordinary rain. It's the Storm — a curse!" The woman seemed more tired than menacing. "In a few days I should head for Manah, perhaps the only safe place. You can sleep here, and then come with me."

Zhi-zi, without realizing it, closed her eyes. She felt the small shoots in her feet wanting to push through her shoes and drive into the soil, to spread roots, to embrace the damp clay.

The trees outside whispered about how fine the climate was, nourished soils and promises of rain.

Even if the Storm was a curse, she would have to leave. She had her own.

When she opened her eyes, she found the innkeeper, whose expression was a blend of concern and bewilderment.

"I cannot stay. I have been here too long already."

***

A sudden gust hurls Fanuskar against the wall. Ahead, the oil lamps flicker; some go out.

Proudly, he contemplates the resilience of the flames left behind, ghostlike and still against the fury of the wind.

The young magic of freshly lit lamps is strong. Were it a common gust, they would sway under the atmospheric pressure. However, this is a supernatural wind, a clash of magics.

Fanuskar fears for those lamps that remain, lit long ago, weakened.

He had never needed to make the journey so fast. His thigh throbs, the recently drawn blood — so constantly, so hastily — sapping his strength.

Still, he pushes forward, braced against the wall, his own weight crushing him in reply to the gale. When he nears a lamp, he snuffs the old flame, collects the fuel from the amphora, shields the flint sparks with his body, and strikes the stone — once, twice, several times — until the flame revives.

Constantly, he talks to himself. "Only two thousand left." "A thousand to go." "Five hundred..."

In a moment of involuntary surrender, preceded by a shiver and a throbbing through his entire leg, Fanuskar's body gives way.

First, his leg stops responding. Then comes the awareness that he will fall. He extends his hands, cushioning the meeting of his face with the ground.

Then comes the after: lying there, he thinks how easy it would be to sleep, how he would wake refreshed, and then leave, never again to draw his own blood.

He recalls the caravans of the displaced, crowding into the plazas of the Manah Empire every summer, like migrant swallows.

"Wretches. I owe them nothing. I owe no one."

Pale and cold, he embraces the weariness, closing his eyes.

***

Zhi-zi felt the mist as a supernatural harbinger.

The previous days had already been dressed in gloom, the Sun obscured by strange purple clouds. Now, however, an absolute darkness swallowed her from every direction, as though it had dissolved every thing and substance in the world into fog and cold, leaving only her.

There were no longer rocks or branches to stumble on. Even the whisper of the trees had fallen silent, replaced by the hiss of the wind and distant thunder.

Zhi-zi was no stranger to storms. She had walked through deserts whose winds carried crystals sharp as arrows. She had crossed mountain ranges whose frozen air solidified the very soul. She had gazed upon electrified skies in hypnotic, hallucinogenic colors.

This was different, she thought. Ancient, profound.

A first drop struck her shoulder, heavy and dense. Soon after, another reached her hand. And then another, and another, until the rain became incessant, a torrent of furious drops.

Amid the curtain of water, Zhi-zi mistook a howl for a clap of thunder, until she felt a jaw scraping her skin, leaving trails of blood. Through her drenched vision, she saw a shadow that had leapt at her back vanish into the fog.

Her breath emptied for one perfect instant, then returned with force.

In a gesture of urgency, she grabbed some seeds from her bag and whispered a hurried ancient tale of saints and dragons. From the seeds sprouted a mother-in-law's tongue, small as a dagger.

When a second shadow emerged ahead, the girl brandished the plant, slicing through it. In its place, others appeared. A swarm watching her from every angle.

It wouldn't have been a difficult fight — the shadows seemed weak — but this was a place of ruin, and Zhi-zi had her own curse. As the rain thickened, bulbs, rhizomes, and roots forced their way out of her body, her curse growing stronger and more uncontrollable.

She needed to keep moving. And fast.

A game began, a dance between endurance and assaults. The shadows danced around her, provoking, delaying, amusing themselves with the trap.

Zhi-zi lost track of time. She tasted blood running down her mouth, from some claw that had just struck her face. Which part of her body was not wounded? She fought against death, shadow after shadow... Tireless, infinite...

Distant points of red light captured Zhi-zi's attention, pulling her from her reverie. She struggled toward them, the light drawing her like a moth.

She was near an enormous wall when the combined luminous warmth of the oil lamps seemed to stretch out and catch her, envelop her, until only she remained — she, the wall, and... a skeletal man lying on the ground, sleeping beside an amphora.

***

For as long as she could remember, Zhi-zi's curse had manifested as an uncontrollable urge for her roots to embrace the soil. She could never stay still; not for long. It had always been so.

Zhi-zi removes her boots and waits for the sensation, the restlessness of her roots. She feels nothing.

She buries her bare feet in the ground, surrenders herself to the earth. No pain, not even an itch.

Her curious gaze settles on the oil lamps. Manah, she thought — perhaps it was a place immune not only to the murderous fogs, but to other curses as well.

For a moment, she observes the man. She studied the flint still clutched in his fist, the amphora resting against his back, the bloodstain spreading through his trousers. She crouches beside him and, next to his aged face, buries a few seeds.

She murmurs, in a tender voice, stories of meetings and partings. She is glad to see that her magic has not died — only the bad part.

From the seeds, white gardenias bloom.

***

"What is this sweet fragrance?" is Fanuskar's first thought upon waking. His consciousness surfaces slowly. His shoulder aches. His leg spasms. His vision is blurred. He had been on the East Gate trail — that much he couldn't forget. But what happened?

In small images that shimmer through his mind, he recalls the gusts of wind, the exhaustion from sleepless nights. Nothing that would explain this scent. His body still feels sluggish, reluctant to rise. Somehow, the floral smell seems to infuse him with energy, exhaling life.

At last, he manages to sit up, opens his eyes, his vision still hazy, and finds the trail... transformed. Filled. What are those white specks?

White flowers, he finally realizes.

Fanuskar stands and examines the plants. They were fresh, new. He was certain he had never seen flowers like these along the trails, and more certain still that they had not been in that same spot the night before.

Without fear, he touches the soft petals. The contact soothes the pain in his hand, reduces the blisters from the burns.

An idea strikes him and, with care, he picks a few petals. He slides them over the wound on his thigh. The pain ceases; the deep cut heals, smooth as porcelain. His skin regains its color.

He manages to gather a good quantity of petals, patiently, so as not to harm the flowers, reserving part of the amphora to store what he has collected.

Had Fanuskar looked at the ground for just a bit longer, he would have noticed that from the flowers there ran a trail of small, bare footprints. But he looks upward, at the dense purple clouds that still linger.

He adjusts the amphora, checking the fuel. After all, there were three hundred lamps to go.

***

Author's Note: This is a longer tale, and a somewhat different one. It had been a while, when I wrote it, since I had written anything in fantasy. I wanted to write about an activity so routine that it loses meaning over time for the person doing it, but whose purpose is renewed if even one person is shown to benefit. I wanted this encounter to be anonymous, fleeting, perceived more through signs than through anything concrete. As encounters tend to be in real life.
