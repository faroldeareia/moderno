The Biomechanic

JULY 1, 2021 • SCIENCE FICTION • SHORT STORY

This story was published, with slight adaptations, in Mysterio magazine, issue #8, in January 2022. @revistamysterioretro

Triton Port, from the days when it was a trading post and final stop for the solar ships, kept only the name and that certain air of decadent urbanity that any port city in history has always flaunted.

Despite having lived here for decades, I never felt comfortable. Not even during the peak of dwarf planet mining, when the ships brought hundreds of potential clients — the desperate kind who would pay any price for a half-decent prosthetic.

Even so, a drink, a dive bar, and a decent view of Neptune. Yeah, that was manageable.

"Tahr Silva," a voice called.

It was not even a question. I turned. The woman at the bar's doorway appeared elderly, well above the life expectancy for a local. I examined her upright posture, the sharp gaze, the stiff movement of her arms. She moved like a soldier — a body trained to carry and control heavy battle suits. All I needed to know was: police or military?

"Yes?" I stared at her.

"Tahr Silva, the biomechanic." She continued, approaching as though she were an old acquaintance greeting me with fondness. I hated that false intimacy.

My profession was no secret. I made a point of everyone knowing my skills with mechanical implants and prosthetics. Everyone knew which bar I used as my office. The place's name, "The Man of the Future," was just one of the ironies and subtleties I enjoyed in life.

But her knowing my name — my real name — could not be a good sign.

"What do you want?" I was brusque.

"Easy, friend." Her false smile was obvious.

She sat across from me and placed an old Class 3B pistol on the table. Too powerful for police use, too old for the military. It must have been one of those weapons used during the War between Terrestrials and Gaseous, decades back, from when the Kuiper centurion patrolled the outer hell.

Still settling into her chair, she continued:

"My name is Joana Hya, private investigator." She projected a holographic card, as if an easily forged gadget proved anything. But I knew it was no lie; her snobbish aura was proof enough. A pain-in-the-ass detective.

"That gun of yours is bloody illegal," I said with disgust.

"Is it? I thought you'd enjoy seeing a memento from your war."

"My war? When the war broke out, I was already buried out here. The dwarf planets didn't take sides, much less me. We were starving, cut off from supplies while you lot played at killing each other."

The woman looked old, and this war talk made no sense to me.

I felt pity thinking she might have gone mad, or else traveled to the edge of civilization chasing a false lead.

Or maybe...

"Did an unhappy client send you? We can work something out, I'm sure. I can't refund any money. As you can see, I'm not exactly swimming in it. But I can do new work, free of labor charge. How's that?"

"The thing is, I'm not worried about the mechanical crap you shove into laborers and criminals. I'm here because of Ali Al'zar."

Ali Al'zar. The famous insurgent. He was responsible for the first attack on the Central Government on Mars, back when he was still the rich, polished leader of a colony on the moon Europa. The spark for an inevitable war.

"Going to deny you knew him?" she added.

I knew denial was pointless, but I didn't want to make things easy.

"What makes you think I knew him?"

"I tracked all his flights, even the most disguised ones. I learned he went to see you on Eris, and a few days later you went to Europa. Simple as that."

"The port security systems on these dwarf planets aren't exactly hard to hack, you know. A camera clearly showed Ali's favorite ship, even disguised, docking right near your clinic."

"Breaking into personal flight logs and port systems. Another bloody illegal thing. And anyway, that's a very weak link. Pure coincidence. You know, sometimes we see what we want to see, but it doesn't really exist."

"Sure. But that's not all. We'll get there, calm down." She seemed proud of her stubbornness in watching infinite hours of port security footage. Then she went on.

"Tell me, then, what do you know about Ali Al'zar?" Her questions sounded like statements.

"What everyone knows. A rich businessman, then a military leader of a rebel colony — now reduced to dust. It's also common knowledge that when he realized his side was losing, he got himself onto an extrasolar ship and vanished like a coward."

"By the time he lands on whatever rock out in the galaxy — if he lands — we'll all be dust ourselves."

"He escaped."

"I know. I'm not after him."

I was growing tired of this game.

"Let's get on with it. Either arrest me or shoot me."

"You're not being accused of anything — not yet. I just want your cooperation. Just a conversation. That's reasonable?"

"About what?" I couldn't deny my curiosity. Ali's name was enough to keep me talking. The bastard had vanished owing me money. And anyway, I had nothing to do with his war — the subject she seemed so interested in.

"I know you met him personally, because you did something for him. Something you yourself may not even know."

Then she rested her hand on the gun. "Let's get on with it, like you said. Tell me how you met Ali. Don't jerk me around."

***

Back then, I was on Eris, the last human frontier in space.

No matter how much science advanced, light-speed travel and the resulting time dilation were still obstacles to the dreamed-of extrasolar exploration. At least on a large scale. There were a few rich or lunatic adventurers who were never heard from again.

I had been expelled from a university on Saturn, theoretically for violating some medical ethics code, and was driven into self-imposed exile. You see, another long-dreamed human conquest had also proven impossible.

Artificial Intelligence existed, but not as we had imagined. We could create an AI, just not one good at multitasking with random variables. We had long been able to build a computer unbeatable at chess, but even a well-trained ape would defeat that same supercomputer at poker.

The solution found for exploring and expanding into the inhospitable solar system was to adapt the human body with biomechanical implants and enhancements. Even so, the body had a limit. If you modified it too much, the human brain would collapse. The nervous system simply could not handle more than two or three radical adaptations. Few had the courage or willingness to test new limits. That, after all, was what they called medical ethics.

Since then, I had a small clinic, living off obscure contracts with methane-mining companies in the dwarf planet region.

That was when a rich man walked into my office.

"Good morning. You are the famous biomechanic of Eris?"

"Not just of Eris, but of all the frozen planets. They call me Max. And you would be...?"

"Ali Al'zar. I own a few prosperous enterprises on Europa and other moons. But I'm afraid I'm not famous anywhere."

You could certainly tell the man had money. He had only one visible implant: his own modified skin.

It was subtle and rare, imperceptible to anyone less trained. Only someone brave — and very rich — would risk being adapted like that.

"Interesting skin you have."

He merely smiled, ironic, and nodded, as though I had answered some question of his.

"What are you doing here, Max? This is a place for fools. People who have already died but forgot about it." His question maintained the ironic smile.

I was obviously offended. Perhaps by the truth of it.

Before I could fire back, he continued.

"And you shouldn't be here either, Max. Or should I say Tahr?"

"How do you know me?"

"I've read your academic papers."

"None of them were published."

"No. Indeed not. But the ethics board that purged you from the scientific community kept a few excerpts in the records. I managed to obtain them. Unfortunately, much had been redacted by censorship. That's why I'm here: to learn what they erased."

I didn't know how he'd gained access to old, supposedly classified documents, but I didn't care. It was the first time anyone had shown interest in my research.

"The research was banned. I never managed to finish it. In fact, even speaking of it would be considered a crime."

"For the Central Government, yes. But why should we worry about their tentacles out here, or in any other colony for that matter? Why do we submit to that control?"

"I don't care about politics."

"Fair enough. But you do care about your research. I can fund and protect you. What do you say? A blank check to finish your science."

I reflected for a moment. The man seemed sincere and eccentric enough for something like this.

"We would need test subjects — human test subjects. Would you be willing to take that step?"

"I can arrange that."

"And we would need a great deal of money. Expensive equipment, rare compounds."

"I can arrange that."

It was an unwritten rule among biomechanics never to ask what certain adaptations would be used for. Generally, it was better not to know. But in this case, I couldn't help myself.

His answer was direct, short, and sounded true: "To set myself free."

Before he left, he placed a chip with some money. It was only a first payment, a sign of good faith, of the total promised — which never came.

Beside the chip, a card in the old style — paper — with an access code for a hidden port on the moon Europa.

***

After I finished my story, Joana projected another holographic card.

This time, showing a multidimensional photo of herself younger, around fifty. She wore the standard battle gear of the Earth–Mars alliance, but with a helmet that indicated high rank. Her identification read colonel, retired. Below her photo was a link to a public medical report, which she did not open. Why was she showing me her ID?

It was not uncommon for retired military personnel to become detectives, bodyguards, or mercenaries. Usually all three at once.

"Did you believe him — Ali?" She stared at me.

"Yes, why wouldn't I? Upon arriving at the moon Europa, I realized there were many skilled engineers working on an extrasolar ship project. I even got to see it — I'd never seen one so large."

"I thought my research was a complementary piece — a way for him to adapt to the voyage. Who knows, maybe even to live floating and dwelling in the vacuum of space. A fully mechanized body could survive without air, without food, without sleep."

"He had even asked about the possibility of a human performing photosynthesis. The potential of my research for an extrasolar explorer was incalculable."

"But you never saw his final adaptation, did you? The result of your work?"

"No. Never. Once the research reached a certain point, the political tension in the gas planets escalated."

"Ali's colony in particular was getting a lot of attention from the Central Government, as you know. For my safety, he asked me to return to the dwarf planets. I think his interests had shifted more toward war than the exploration of the universe."

"The money I watched run out, but that was fine — my research had advanced."

She seemed pensive and looked at me differently than she had until then. Still as though she were an old friend, she signaled the bar and ordered two shots.

"You told me your past. Let me tell you mine."

***

There was a coordinated attack against several bases on Mars.

It was not the first attempted revolt by some minor colony. But the success of the attack was staggering.

At the time, I was an Intelligence Officer, and the survivors' accounts were almost always the same: androids, intelligent robots, thinking machines.

In the first, second, and third reports from our survivors, we assumed it was some confusion on their part, some trauma or mistake. Rumors from frightened soldiers. Perhaps even a deliberate enemy ploy — a form of deception meant to terrify our ranks.

But the attacks did not stop, and our casualties were staggering.

The war had spread everywhere; colonies that had been submissive until then seized upon our weakness and joined in a grand alliance. The most astonishing part was that we could never truly understand what was happening.

I was finally ordered to investigate the enemy's technology, and I needed to devise a maneuver for that.

"General Hya will explain the mission," a high-ranking marshal announced in an Intelligence War Room.

"The objective is to capture the enemy's technology and understand what is actually going on," I began.

"We will set a trap on Phobos. We'll simulate a false shipyard there, with fake hulls of nonexistent vessels, leading the enemy to believe we have a new naval technology in production. We'll let just enough slip to make them curious."

"The satellite is very close to Mars, preventing a direct approach by the enemy fleet, so they will likely send small, agile transports to deploy a ground team and bring the fight to the surface."

Another colonel raised his hand, signaling he wished to interject.

"General Hya, it's precisely in ground combat that we've been losing! It would be better to keep the fight at the naval level. Perhaps intercept their transport before landing."

"Actually, we've tried something similar before, and they self-destructed."

I felt a sharp silence in the room. The thought of suicidal soldiers was hard to swallow. We were dealing with revolted colonies, not lunatics. Perhaps, deep down, everyone thought it possible that we were indeed at war with androids.

I did not believe that. It had already been proven that so-called Artificial Intelligence did not exist.

"I believe," I continued, explaining my plan, "that the enemy has developed some modern, compact, and highly efficient battle suit. The problem with suits is their size, the energy cost. They've somehow overcome that."

"But suits are suits. Without them, on an airless rock in space, the enemy will suffocate. And a suffocating enemy cannot self-destruct — not even the most fanatical."

I could sense a hopeful attention upon me.

"We will launch EMP missiles from Mars — enough to fry any equipment that depends on energy, any device through which an electron passes. The more modern a suit, the more it depends on power. Well, that's the theory."

"Even the legs and arms of modern suits have electronic assistance to offset the weight. An electromagnetic pulse would render any modern soldier immobile."

"And wouldn't this pulse also paralyze our own soldiers' suits?" someone asked the obvious question I had been expecting.

"Not if they're mechanical suits, without electricity."

"Space diving suits, you mean?" Someone cracked a joke, drawing a few laughs.

I was serious.

"Precisely. Space diving suits."

I knew the plan was risky. We had no certainty the pulse could burn those theoretical enemy suits. It was a genuine lottery. And I would not force any soldier to risk wearing obsolete suits against definitively more modern enemies.

So I volunteered, with my team, for the mission.

We were in Phobos's orbit, holed up in craters, like archaic trench soldiers, wearing suits that basically depended on oxygen cylinders.

We knew that once the enemy breached the orbital defense and hit the ground, Mars would bombard us with pulse missiles. Any communication, any functioning electronic equipment, would cease at that moment.

The instant we saw a small transport hover not far ahead, and enemy soldiers leap from it — unconcerned with the feeble anti-aircraft fire we simulated — a missile at incalculable speed, launched from Mars, detonated between us. Silent, dark.

The ground shook; a wave of energy passed through us like a shiver, shutting down our communicators, wrist computers, and even flashlights.

An enormous cloud of dust — sand, pebbles, and rocks — floated between us and the enemy, hovering in the low gravity. We had only our eyesight.

I felt blind without the aid of infrared, without the targeting computer or the sonar.

We lit gas lanterns on our helmets, like ancient miners, and went toward the enemy.

We were armed only with old powder weapons, the kind found only in museums. The idea was to shoot any surviving enemy soldier, praying their modern weapons were nonfunctional and that our lead bullets would pierce whatever armor they wore.

In a way, the EMP missile plan worked, and the enemy seemed immobilized in most of their functions. But not entirely.

What I saw seemed impossible.

Some enemies were crouching, clutching their heads; some were rolling on the ground; others were disoriented, punching themselves. I could see in their metal faces — which, up close, looked nothing like helmets but rather fused visages — a failed attempt at an expression of horror.

Had there been atmosphere, I would have been certain I could hear desperate screams.

Those who had more mobility, those who had some functioning weapon, took their own lives. Androids do not kill themselves like that. Not as though death were a liberation.

Those were people, transformed into machines.

***

After the account, the detective stared at me like an accusation. I had to ask her.

"Did you manage to capture anyone?"

"Yes. My colleagues seemed terrified, frozen. Only I had the strength to finish the mission. I captured one enemy and killed the rest. I didn't think of it as a massacre, but as mercy."

"Afterward, reinforcements arrived."

"I don't know what happened from then on."

"Let me guess — you were discharged from the military?"

She nodded.

"I had told my superiors that the enemies were not androids with artificial intelligence, nor were they wearing supermodern suits."

"They were people, transformed into machines and fused with their own suits and other technologies. By that point, our labs should have been saying the same. My superiors merely thanked me for my years of service, and a psychological evaluation forced my retirement. Apparently, war trauma had led me to create hallucinations."

The strange thing was that, despite everything, I did not feel as though she wanted revenge on me. I felt I needed to say something, and I was sincere.

"Honestly, I didn't know my research would lead to this. I don't know if that helps absolve my guilt. Even the test subjects we used were only people with terminal illnesses. They could never have been used to become soldiers. I thought my research was for space exploration, not war."

"Yes, I believe you." And she truly did.

"Before, I didn't know how aware you were of how your research was being used. I knew someone had helped Ali, and I knew he came here looking for a biomechanic. I went after other records of yours but didn't find much. Even the data from your expulsion, the ethics trial, had been deleted."

"The Central Government must have erased them," I inferred.

"I believe it was Ali himself. Otherwise, the Central Government would have already come after you to erase you too."

"Well, you should be pleased. Now your military has the technology. Strange they haven't used it yet."

"We don't know. The war took another turn after my mission. But I don't feel proud. It doesn't matter which side has the technology — the terror of that day haunts me. I sometimes dream they turn me into a robot without consciousness. To this day I don't know what really happened. How did you manage to adapt the human body so much?"

So that was it. Decades following my trail, for one simple answer.

"I used mental locks — a form of implant, actually rather simple, that suppresses the brain's self-awareness. But that's why the research was never viable: it always ended up removing the patient's free will. Who would agree to do that to themselves? I never imagined that the gap left by free will could be filled with military orders."

"The missile's pulse must have shut down those locks, and some bodily mobility remained possible because parts of the original nervous system and limb muscles had not been fully replaced."

"I waited for her to process the explanation. "So — now that you know, are you going to kill me?"

"You were a pariah. Your research was heinous and resulted in even more terror. But the only way to prevent it from being used again is to make it public."

"You want me to expose my research to the public?"

"Yes. In a way, it will be the recognition you never received. You'll be able to prove you were right, after all."

"The Central Government might even cut deals with you, for God's sake."

I studied her. I didn't know if she wanted revenge on the military that kicked her out, or simply to close a cycle of nightmares. But of one thing I was certain: regardless of her vendetta against the Central Government, she would also become famous.

"It'll be recognition for you too, detective. I imagine exposing a crime of this magnitude will increase your client base."

An elderly detective with a negative psychiatric record stamped across her own ID couldn't have much of a client roster.

She ignored my comment.

"Come on, there's a journalist waiting for us in another bar." She finally holstered her weapon and stood.

The detective and I left The Man of the Future together, like old friends who shared a single secret.

***

Author's Note: This story was strongly influenced by Alastair Reynolds, an author I discovered through the series "Love, Death + Robots." Many people told me it reminded them of "The Expanse," though I had neither watched the show nor read the books when I wrote this particular story. I watched the series later, and I enjoyed it very much. I was quite pleased by the comparison. The idea of expansion through the universe while maintaining technology that isn't too advanced has always been a form of fiction that appeals to me. Shows like Firefly, The Expanse, and other "hard sci-fi."
